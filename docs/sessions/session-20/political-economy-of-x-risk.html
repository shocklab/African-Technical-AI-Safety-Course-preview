Week 10 • Session 20.2

The political economy of the x-risk frame

Where the existential-risk framing comes from, whose interests it serves, and why that does not by itself make it false

What we'll cover

The previous sub-session questioned whether the field understands its object. This one questions its motives. Three connected arguments: that the drive to build "AGI" descends from a specific ideological lineage with troubling roots (the TESCREAL critique); that AI is better understood as a normal technology than as an approaching singularity (Narayanan and Kapoor); and that doom narratives, whatever their truth, advance the commercial interests of the firms that promote them (the AI Now critique). Each is steelmanned. The sub-session closes on how to weigh an argument about who benefits from a belief against the question of whether the belief is true.

TESCREAL: where the frame comes from

Gebru and Torres (2024) argue that the AGI project carries an ideological inheritance, and that naming it changes how you read the safety discourse.

In "The TESCREAL bundle", Timnit Gebru and Émile Torres trace the pursuit of artificial general intelligence to a cluster of overlapping ideologies: Transhumanism, Extropianism, Singularitarianism, Cosmism, Rationalism, Effective Altruism, and Longtermism. Their claim is genealogical and normative at once: that this bundle descends in part from twentieth-century eugenic thinking, and that its vision (a glorious, vast future secured by building god-like AI) licenses a particular move. If the stakes are literally cosmic, then present harms look small by comparison, and a lab can invoke the language of "safety" and "benefiting humanity" while deflecting accountability for the bias, labour exploitation and power concentration it produces today. Their prescription is to abandon the undefined goal of "AGI" — which, being undefined, can never be shown to be safe — in favour of well-scoped systems built for stated tasks with real safety protocols. You do not have to accept the genealogy to feel the force of the structural point: an unfalsifiable, infinitely large stake is a convenient thing for an incumbent to wield.

AI as a normal technology

Narayanan and Kapoor reject the dichotomy between utopia and catastrophe, and ask you to treat AI like the other general-purpose technologies we have absorbed.

In AI Snake Oil and the essay "AI as Normal Technology", Arvind Narayanan and Sayash Kapoor argue that AI is a general-purpose technology that will diffuse through society over decades, gated by the slow pace at which institutions adopt and adapt to it, much as electricity and the internet did. On this view both the imminent-superintelligence story and the naive-utopia story are mistakes of the same kind: they imagine a sudden discontinuity where history suggests gradual, contested, uneven change. Much of what is sold as predictive AI, they argue, simply does not work as advertised, and generative AI, while real, is oversold; the nearer dangers are human misuse and the concentration of corporate power, not an autonomous system slipping its leash. The policy implication is to build resilience — institutions that can absorb and correct AI's failures — rather than to organise everything around preventing a feared takeover.

The incumbents' interest

The AI Now Institute makes the political-economy point directly: the inevitability narrative is an asset to the firms that tell it.

AI Now's 2025 report Artificial Power (Kate Brennan, Amba Kak and Sarah Myers West) names what it calls "the AGI mythology": the way "AGI" collapses a messy technical reality into "a singular, imminent, and inevitable future that conveniently advances the interests of the companies claiming to build it." Inevitability and doom, on this reading, are not neutral forecasts but framings that foreclose scrutiny — if catastrophe is coming and only the frontier labs can prevent it, then the frontier labs should be trusted, resourced, and lightly regulated. Meredith Whittaker has put the same point more bluntly in interviews, calling the existential-risk stories "advertisements" that draw attention away from the concentrated corporate power that is the real present concern. The argument is not that the people raising existential risk are insincere; it is that sincerity is beside the point when a narrative happens to serve the teller.

How to weigh a "who benefits" argument

These critiques are about evidence, resources and politics at once, and they need handling with care. That a belief is convenient for powerful actors is a reason to scrutinise it, not a proof that it is false; the genetic fallacy is to think otherwise. A safety risk could be real and cynically exploited by incumbents; both can hold. So the honest reading keeps two questions apart. One is whose interests the framing serves, which the political-economy critics answer well and a frontier researcher should sit with rather than dismiss. The other is whether the technical risk is real, which the incentives of the people discussing it cannot settle either way. The course's position is to take the political-economy critique seriously as a check on motivated reasoning and regulatory capture, while still assessing the technical arguments of Weeks 2 to 8 on their own evidence.

Questions to bring to class

  • State the TESCREAL critique's structural point without relying on the genealogy. Is the structural point weakened if you reject the historical claim?
  • "AI as normal technology" rejects both doom and utopia. What observation in the next five years would count as evidence against it?
  • Distinguish "this framing serves incumbents" from "this framing is false". Give a case where the first is true and the second is not.
  • If you were a frontier-safety researcher who found the AI Now critique partly right, what would you change about how you work or communicate?

Readings

Core

• Gebru, T. & Torres, É. P. (2024), "The TESCREAL bundle: Eugenics and the promise of utopia through artificial general intelligence" (First Monday 29(4)) — the ideological-lineage argument.

• Narayanan, A. & Kapoor, S. (2025), "AI as Normal Technology" (Knight First Amendment Institute) — the gradualist alternative to both doom and utopia.

Supplementary

• AI Now Institute (2025), Artificial Power: 2025 Landscape Report (ainowinstitute.org) — "the AGI mythology" and the concentration-of-power argument.

• Narayanan, A. & Kapoor, S. (2024), AI Snake Oil (Princeton University Press) — the book-length case for telling AI's real capabilities from its marketing.
