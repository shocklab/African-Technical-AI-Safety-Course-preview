Features, directions, and superposition

Why a model represents more features than it has dimensions, why sparsity is what permits it, and why that is the reason interpretability is hard

What we'll cover

If you want to read a model's internals, you need to know what the unit of meaning is. The working hypothesis is that it is a feature, represented as a direction in activation space. This sub-session states that hypothesis precisely, then confronts the fact that makes interpretability hard: models pack more features than they have dimensions, a trick called superposition. We derive why that is geometrically possible (the almost-orthogonal count), why sparsity is the condition that makes it worthwhile, and we solve the smallest non-trivial case in closed form to expose a sharp phase change. The consequence (polysemantic neurons) is the obstacle the whole field is built to get around.

The linear representation hypothesis

Features are directions; an activation is (approximately) a weighted sum of the feature directions that are active.

Write the activation vector at some layer as \(a \in \mathbb{R}^n\). The hypothesis has two parts. Decomposability: the model represents a set of human-meaningful features, and at any input some subset of them is "active." Linearity: each feature \(i\) has a direction \(d_i \in \mathbb{R}^n\), and the activation is the sum of the active features along their directions,

\[ a \;\approx\; \sum_i f_i(\text{input})\, d_i, \]

where \(f_i \ge 0\) is how strongly feature \(i\) fires. The features \(f_i\) are arbitrary nonlinear functions of the input; what is linear is the map from features to the activation. This is a strong, falsifiable claim, and the evidence for it is good but not total: it is the reason "steering vectors" (add a direction to make a model more honest, Session 16) and linear probes work at all. Treat it as the field's central working hypothesis, not a theorem.

Privileged vs non-privileged bases

A basis is privileged if the architecture gives its coordinate axes special meaning. An element-wise nonlinearity does that: \(\mathrm{ReLU}\) acts coordinate-by-coordinate, so rotating the basis changes the function. After an MLP's activation, then, the neuron directions are privileged, and asking "is this neuron interpretable?" is a sensible question. The residual stream, by contrast, has no element-wise nonlinearity acting directly on it, so it has no privileged basis: features there can point in any direction and need not align with coordinates at all. This is the first reason you cannot "read off neurons": in a non-privileged basis there are no neurons to read, only directions.

Superposition: more features than dimensions

A model can store many more features than it has dimensions by accepting a little interference.

If every feature needed its own orthogonal direction, an \(n\)-dimensional space could hold only \(n\) features. The superposition hypothesis is that models do better than this: they represent many more than \(n\) features by placing them along directions that are merely almost orthogonal, tolerating the small overlaps (interference) that result. The geometry makes this astonishingly cheap.

The almost-orthogonal count (why superposition is possible at all)

In \(\mathbb{R}^n\) you can fit only \(n\) mutually orthogonal unit vectors. But if you relax "orthogonal" to "nearly orthogonal" (pairwise \(|\langle d_i, d_j\rangle| < \varepsilon\)), the count explodes. The Johnson–Lindenstrauss lemma gives the scaling: the number of such vectors you can pack is

\[ N \;=\; \exp\!\big(\Theta(\varepsilon^2 n)\big), \]

i.e. exponential in the dimension. Concretely, a few thousand dimensions can hold not a few thousand but astronomically many directions whose pairwise cosine is under, say, \(0.1\). So the question is never "can the model fit extra features?" (geometrically it obviously can), but "is it worth the interference?" That is what sparsity decides.

Sparsity is the driver

Superposition pays off when features are rare, because then the interference rarely bites.

Two features placed on overlapping directions interfere only when they are both active at once. If features are sparse (each fires only occasionally, and so two given features rarely co-occur), then the interference term is usually absent, and on the rare collision the nonlinearity can clean up small spurious activations. So a sparse world rewards packing many features in superposition; a dense world (features usually on) punishes it, and the model is better off representing only its \(n\) most important features orthogonally. Real features are extremely sparse (most concepts are absent from most inputs), which is why superposition is the rule in real models. The way to see the trade-off flip is to solve the smallest case exactly.

A closed-form phase change: two features, one dimension

Take the toy model \(x' = \mathrm{ReLU}(W^\top W x + b)\), which compresses \(m\) features through \(n < m\) dimensions and tries to reconstruct them, trained under an importance-weighted loss \(L = \sum_i I_i\,(x_i - x'_i)^2\). Take the smallest interesting case: \(m = 2\) features, \(n = 1\) dimension, so \(W = [w_1\ \ w_2]\) is a single row. There are essentially two qualitatively distinct solutions:

  • Keep one feature, drop the other: \(W = [1,\,0]\) (or \([0,1]\)). Perfect on the kept feature, blind to the other.
  • Antipodal superposition: \(W = [1,\,-1]\) (up to scale). Both features share the one dimension on opposite signs, each recoverable when alone, but they collide if both fire.

Which wins depends on the activation probability \(p\), the chance that a feature is active on a given input. (Elhage et al. define sparsity the other way round, as \(S = 1 - p\), the probability a feature is zero: small \(p\) means high sparsity.) When features are dense (\(p\) near 1) collisions are constant, the interference dominates, and dropping a feature is optimal. When features are sparse (\(p\) small) collisions are rare, the expected interference cost falls below the cost of being blind to a whole feature, and antipodal superposition wins. The switch between the two regimes is not gradual. Elhage et al. confirm analytically that it is a first-order phase change: as you lower \(p\) past a threshold, the optimal \(W\) jumps discontinuously from the orthogonal solution to the superposed one. This is one of the rare interpretability results with an exact closed form, and it makes the abstract claim concrete: superposition is an optimal response to sparsity.

Scaled up, superposition organises into striking geometry: features arrange themselves into regular structures (antipodal pairs, triangles, pentagons, tetrahedra) that are solutions to a packing problem, the same Thomson-problem shapes that minimise interference among many directions. The details are beautiful but matter less for safety than the fact that comes next.

Polysemanticity

Superposition means a single neuron participates in many unrelated features. That is why you cannot read it.

If features outnumber neurons and share directions, then any one neuron lights up for a grab-bag of unrelated concepts: it is polysemantic. This is the observable symptom of superposition and the central obstacle to interpretability: you cannot understand a model by reading its neurons one at a time, because no neuron means one thing. Even similarity-based probes can mislead, because the direction you find may be a blend. Everything difficult about mechanistic interpretability traces back here. It is also why Session 15 spends its effort on sparse autoencoders, whose entire purpose is to undo superposition: to take the polysemantic activation and re-express it in an over-complete basis of (hopefully) monosemantic features. Session 13 establishes the disease; Session 15 examines the proposed cure, and Session 16 asks whether it works.

Questions to bring to class

  • State the linear representation hypothesis in your own words, separating decomposability from linearity. Which one does a steering vector rely on?
  • Why does an element-wise nonlinearity create a privileged basis, while the residual stream has none? What does that imply for "interpreting a neuron" in each location?
  • Derive (or argue) why the number of near-orthogonal directions grows exponentially with dimension. Why does that make "can the model fit more features?" the wrong question?
  • In the two-features-one-dimension model, explain in terms of expected interference why high sparsity (rarely-active features) favours superposition. What does "first-order phase change" add beyond "the optimum changes"?
