Week 7 • Session 13.1

Why open the black box?

What mechanistic interpretability is, how it differs from saliency and from black-box evaluation, and the state of the reverse-engineering ambition

What we'll cover

Every safety number in this course so far has been read off the outside of a model: a refusal rate, a benchmark score, an attack-success rate. Mechanistic interpretability asks a different question: what is the model actually computing inside, and can we read it the way we read source code? This sub-session defines the field, places it inside the evaluation taxonomy from Session 11, and sets out from the start how far the ambition currently reaches: convincingly for one- and two-layer toy models, far more tentatively for frontier systems. The rest of Session 13 earns the tools; here we set the question and the standard of evidence.

Three ways to explain a model's behaviour

Interpretability is not one thing. The useful split is by what kind of explanation you are after.

Take a single concrete behaviour: a language model, shown a repeated sequence, correctly continues it. There are three levels at which you could "explain" that, and they are not interchangeable.

Levels of explanation

  • Black-box / behavioural. Measure the output: how often does the model complete the sequence correctly, and how does that vary with the input? This is what every eval in Session 11 did. It tells you what the model does, never why.
  • Attribution / saliency. Ask which inputs mattered: gradient-based saliency maps, feature-importance scores, probing classifiers trained on activations. This localises where the relevant information is, but it still treats the computation as opaque and is notoriously easy to over-read.
  • Mechanistic. Reverse-engineer the actual algorithm: which attention head, via which weight matrices, moves which information, such that you could in principle re-implement the behaviour by hand. This is the level that says why, and it is what "mechanistic interpretability" means.

The course's claim is that only the third level eventually answers the safety questions the others cannot: not "did the model behave on our test set" but "what is it computing, and would that computation generalise or betray us off-distribution". Sessions 13–16 pursue the third level and note how often we reach it.

Interpretability is the white-box arm of evaluation

Session 11.1 already drew the line we need; interpretability lives on one side of it.

Recall the taxonomy from 11.1: evaluations are behavioural (read inputs and outputs only) or internal (read activations and weights). Mechanistic interpretability is the toolkit that makes the internal side possible. This matters because the course has repeatedly hit the limits of behavioural evidence. In Session 10.4, a black-box unlearning test certified hazardous knowledge as gone that a white-box probe found still present. In Session 11.2, a model can sandbag (look incapable while being capable), and distinguishing genuine inability from hidden capability needs to look inside. Interpretability is the response to a recurring problem: behaviour can be made to look however a capable model wants it to look, so a safety case that rests only on behaviour is a safety case a sufficiently capable model can defeat. Reading the mechanism is what behaviour-faking cannot directly counter.

The reverse-engineering ambition

The dream is to read a network like a decompiled program. The reality today is closer to neuroscience than to decompilation.

The strong form of the ambition is striking: a trained network is just a large pile of matrices and nonlinearities, fully observable, perfectly reproducible, with every activation available for inspection and every component open to intervention. In principle nothing is hidden, which is what a neuroscientist studying a brain would envy. The field has delivered this for small systems. As Sessions 13.3–13.4 will show, a one-layer attention-only transformer can be written out almost completely as a set of token-to-token tables read directly from its weights, and a two-layer model's in-context-learning circuit can be reverse-engineered and verified by ablation.

The evidence at frontier scale

For frontier models the case is far weaker, and the framing comes from the interpretability researchers themselves: at scale the evidence is more like neuroscience (lesion studies, developmental observations, perturbations) than like reading source code. Results that are causal for small attention-only models (established by tens of thousands of targeted ablations) are usually only correlational for large models with MLPs. The course will grade evidence this way throughout the block: a tidy mechanistic story on a toy model is strong; the same story extrapolated to a 70-billion-parameter model is a hypothesis until tested. Treat anyone who tells you interpretability has "solved" transparency for frontier models with the scepticism Session 1 trained into you.

The safety payoffs

Interpretability is a means to an end; its safety value is what it might let us do that nothing else can.

Three payoffs motivate the block, each of which Session 16 examines critically rather than takes on faith. It could let us detect deception: read whether a model internally "believes" something other than what it says, the open problem left hanging in Session 3.4. It could underwrite scalable oversight (Session 8.1) by giving a supervisor a window the model cannot fake. And it could make auditing meaningful: a regulator or a deploying institution with a "right to inspect" (Session 12) needs tools that work on the model artefact itself. Whether interpretability actually delivers any of these at the scale that matters is contested, and we defer that account to Session 16. For now these payoffs are large enough to justify the technical work that follows.

The African-lens angle

This is the part of the course where the African lens is thinnest. The angle, developed in Session 16, is sovereignty: interpretability is the one safety tool that operates on a model's weights without needing the developer's training data or cooperation, which makes it the natural instrument for an institution that receives a frontier model rather than building it. Whether the features and circuits a model learns are shared across languages, or specific to the high-resource ones, is a real and open question with direct consequences for isiZulu and other low-resource languages. Both matter; the foundations in 13.2–13.4 are language- and geography-neutral mathematics.

Questions to bring to class

  • Give a safety question that a black-box evaluation cannot answer even in principle, and say what a mechanistic explanation would add.
  • Why is attribution (saliency, probing) not the same as mechanistic understanding? Construct a case where a saliency map is correct about where and wrong about why.
  • Interpretability researchers describe their frontier-scale evidence as more like neuroscience than like decompilation. What does that analogy concede, and which Session-11 failure does it echo?
  • Of the three safety payoffs (deception detection, scalable oversight, auditing), which most depends on interpretability working at frontier scale rather than toy scale? Why?
