Induction heads and in-context learning

A two-head algorithm for copying patterns, and the leading mechanistic account of in-context learning

What we'll cover

An induction head is a concrete, reverse-engineered circuit that completes patterns it has seen earlier in the context, and it may be the main mechanism behind in-context learning, the ability of large models to pick up a task from examples in the prompt with no weight updates. We build it from the QK/OV algebra of 13.3: a two-head, K-composition circuit whose pieces we can write down and whose behaviour the Session 14 lab will detect and verify by ablation. We also weigh the evidence with care (strong and causal for small models, suggestive and correlational at scale) and meet the training-time phase change that connects this circuit to the emergence debate of Session 2.4.

The induction behaviour

Given …[A][B]…[A], an induction head predicts [B]: "what followed A last time follows it again."

Feed a model a sequence with a repeated pattern: some token \(A\) was once followed by \(B\), and now \(A\) appears again. An induction head completes it with \(B\). It is doing something contextual: \(A\) and \(B\) can be arbitrary, even random tokens the model never saw in training, so this is an in-context rule learned on the fly rather than a memorised bigram. Mechanistically this takes two heads working in series, which is why it cannot exist in a one-layer model (13.3).

The two-head circuit

  • Head 1: a previous-token head (early layer). At each position it attends one step back and copies the previous token's identity forward, writing "the token before me was \(X\)" into a subspace of the residual stream at the current position.
  • Head 2: the induction head (later layer), via K-composition. Its key circuit reads the subspace head 1 wrote. So the current token \(A\) (as query) matches the key at the position that records being preceded by \(A\), which is the position right after the earlier occurrence of \(A\), namely the position holding \(B\). Having attended there, the induction head's OV circuit copies \(B\) to the output, raising its logit.

Head 1 stamps each position with its predecessor; head 2 uses its query token to find the position stamped with that same token, and copies whatever sits there. Prefix-matching (a QK job) plus copying (an OV job), composed across two layers.

Writing the two pieces down

Both halves reduce to eigenvalue statements about the gauge-invariant circuits from 13.3.

OV = copying ⇔ positive eigenvalues

A head copies the token it attends to when its OV circuit, viewed as a map from input token to output logit, preserves token identity. Compose the OV circuit with the embedding and unembedding to get a token-by-token matrix \(W_U\, W_{OV}\, W_E\). The head copies when this matrix is positive along its diagonal: token \(t\) in raises the logit of token \(t\) out. Operationally, define a copying score as the magnitude-weighted fraction of positive eigenvalues of \(W_U W_{OV} W_E\), \(\sum_i \lambda_i \big/ \sum_i |\lambda_i|\): a pure copying head scores near \(+1\). This is the OV half, and it is checkable directly from the weights; note, though, that Olsson et al.'s primary evaluator for classifying heads is behavioural, measured on repeated random sequences, with the eigenvalue statistic as the weights-based check.

QK = prefix matching via K-composition

The induction head's key circuit reads the previous-token information head 1 wrote. The effective QK form is no longer just \(W_{QK}\) on token embeddings but \(W_{QK}\) composed with head 1's \(W_{OV}\) in the key slot: a cross-term that scores "does the key position record being preceded by my query token?" highest. Operationally, define a prefix-matching score: run a repeated random sequence and measure, for each position in the repeat, how much attention the candidate head pays back to the token that followed the current token's earlier occurrence. A genuine induction head puts almost all its attention there. These two scores, copying and prefix-matching, are the detectors you will implement in the Session 14 lab.

The prefix-matching erratum

The prefix-matching definition in Olsson et al. was corrected after publication (the change log records flipping "preceded by" and "followed by" in the description). It is a small thing, but a useful reminder that even canonical, careful interpretability work ships errata: read the definitions, not the summary, and check them against the behaviour. The same scepticism the course applies to vendor claims applies to interpretability papers, including the famous ones.

The in-context-learning claim

These heads may be the mechanism behind much of in-context learning, and the evidence must be graded by model size.

The striking hypothesis from Olsson et al. is that induction heads are the main driver of in-context learning in transformers. The evidence is presented as several converging lines, and this course grades them rather than accepting the claim wholesale:

The evidence, graded

  • Strong / causal: small attention-only models. Here induction heads can be identified exactly and knocked out by ablation; doing so destroys in-context learning. This was established with tens of thousands of targeted head ablations. For these models the claim is causal.
  • Suggestive / correlational: large models with MLPs. At scale the same heads are present and correlate with in-context-learning ability, and perturbing the architecture moves both together (a co-occurrence argument). But the clean causal isolation of the small-model case is not available, so the claim here is a well-supported hypothesis, not a proof.

Carry this grading habit through the whole block: "demonstrated on a two-layer attention-only model" and "holds in GPT-scale models" are different epistemic states, and good interpretability work tells you which one it has.

The training phase change

Induction heads form abruptly during training, a phase change you can see in the loss curve.

One of the more striking findings is that induction heads do not fade in gradually: they form in a sharp phase change during training, visible as a small bump in the loss curve, co-occurring with an abrupt jump in the model's in-context-learning score. This recalls two things the course has already met. It echoes the superposition phase change of 13.2: capabilities and representations both seem to switch on discontinuously as a threshold is crossed. And it connects to the emergence debate of Session 2.4: here is a case where a real, mechanistically-identified capability appears abruptly in training, which is a more grounded claim than emergence read off a benchmark curve (and reminds us, per Schaeffer, to check whether the abruptness is in the model or the metric). For safety the implication is unwelcome: capabilities, and the circuits behind them, can arrive suddenly rather than on a smooth, forecastable ramp.

The African-lens thread: induction heads translate

Olsson et al. note that the same heads do fuzzy matching, including word-by-word translation: given [A*][B*]…[A] where \(A^*\) is \(A\) in another language, the head completes with \(B\), the translation of \(B^*\). That is a small but real piece of evidence that some circuits are language-agnostic: the copying-and-matching machinery does not care which language the tokens are in. It raises the open question we return to in Session 16: if the mechanisms are language-neutral but the features they operate on are densely represented only for high-resource languages, then capability may transfer to isiZulu while reliability does not. The 2025 attribution-graph work (15.4) found this split at frontier scale: Claude 3.5 Haiku computes with a mixture of language-independent concept circuits and language-specific output circuits. A testable interpretability question with direct relevance to the course's anchor, and a ready honours-project seed.

Questions to bring to class

  • Walk through the two-head induction circuit and say exactly which job (prefix-matching vs copying) is done by which circuit (QK vs OV) of which head. Why is K-composition the link?
  • Why does a head copy iff \(W_U W_{OV} W_E\) has positive eigenvalues? What does the copying score measure, and how would you compute it from weights alone?
  • The claim that induction heads drive in-context learning is "causal for small models, correlational at scale." What specific experiment makes it causal for small models, and why can't you run the equivalent on a frontier model?
  • Induction heads form in a training phase change. How is that a stronger or weaker emergence claim than the benchmark-curve "emergence" of Session 2.4?

Readings

Core

• Olsson et al. (2022), "In-context Learning and Induction Heads" (arXiv:2209.11895): the induction-head mechanism, the phase change, and the (carefully graded) in-context-learning argument.

Supplementary

• Nanda et al. (2023), "Progress Measures for Grokking via Mechanistic Interpretability" (arXiv:2301.05217): the only fully closed-form circuit (modular addition via Fourier multiplication); pairs beautifully with the phase-change theme if you want a second worked example.
