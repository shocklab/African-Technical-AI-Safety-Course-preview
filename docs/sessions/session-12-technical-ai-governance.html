Week 6 • Session 12

🏛️ Technical AI Governance

The technical mechanisms — assessment, access, verification, security — that could make safety policy enforceable, and what they look like from Africa

🎯 What we'll cover

Session 11 built evaluations: ways to measure what a model can do and whether it will. This session asks the next question — given a measurement, who acts on it, and how would anyone verify compliance? That is technical AI governance: not law in the abstract, but the engineering substrate that determines whether a governance proposal is enforceable or merely aspirational.

We use a clean map of the field, locate compute governance on it (callback to Session 4), compare the three real frontier-safety frameworks labs operate under, touch agent governance, and then look at the whole question from an African vantage point — where the RIA Just AI framework, the AU strategy, POPIA, and the sovereignty debate change which levers are even available.

🧩 A map: capacities × targets

Reuel et al.'s "Open Problems in Technical AI Governance" gives the field a two-dimensional taxonomy. Almost every concrete proposal is a cell in this grid.

The taxonomy

Capacity (what you do)What it means
AssessmentMeasuring capabilities, risks, and impacts (evals, audits, red-teaming).
AccessStructured, staged access for researchers/auditors without handing over everything.
VerificationConfirming claims are true — that a lab trained what it says, within agreed limits.
SecurityProtecting model weights and infrastructure from theft or tampering.
OperationalisationTurning fuzzy benchmarks into thresholds a regulator can actually use.

Each capacity is applied across four targets along the value chain: data · compute · models/algorithms · deployment. "Compute verification via on-chip mechanisms" is one cell; "deployment assessment via evals" is another. The grid is a good way to locate — and to scope — a governance project.

⚡ Compute governance — why it's the tractable lever

🔌 Back to the substrate

Recall Session 4: capability ← compute ← chips + energy. Compute is excludable, quantifiable, and detectable in a way data and algorithms are not. That is precisely why it anchors the most concrete governance proposals — training-compute thresholds, export controls on advanced chips and fabrication equipment, and research into on-chip mechanisms that could make a chip's use verifiable.

It is also the lever with the sharpest equity edge: controlling compute controls who can build frontier systems at all — which lands very differently depending on where you sit.

📋 The three frontier-safety frameworks

The leading labs publish voluntary policies that share a common skeleton: capability thresholds → eval commitments → required mitigations → a commitment to pause if mitigations aren't ready.

Anthropic — RSP

The Responsible Scaling Policy defines AI Safety Levels (ASL); crossing a capability threshold triggers specified security and deployment safeguards.

OpenAI — Preparedness

Tracks risk categories at Low/Medium/High/Critical tiers, gating deployment and further development on scorecards.

DeepMind — Frontier Safety Framework

Defines "critical capability levels" and the mitigations expected when models approach them.

💡 Read them together, not separately

METR's "Common Elements of Frontier AI Safety Policies" distils what these share and where they differ. The instructive class exercise (Session 12 seminar) is a jigsaw: groups take one framework each, then cross-compare against the "Common Elements" — what's shared, what's missing, and crucially what's enforceable versus self-reported? All three are voluntary, which is itself the governance gap.

🤖 Agent governance

💡 New surface area

As systems become agentic — taking actions, using tools, running for long horizons (Weeks 5 and the Session 22 agents material) — governance has to cover not just models but actions: identifiers for AI agents, logging and rollback, liability for autonomous actions, and limits on what an agent may do without a human in the loop. This is among the least-settled corners of the field and a rich source of conceptual projects.

🌍 Governance from Africa

The taxonomy is universal; the available levers are not. From a South African vantage point the picture shifts.

Frameworks, law, and the sovereignty question

The AU Continental AI Strategy (2024) lists "minimise risks" among its focus areas and explicitly flags data-centre water use — a continental framework, though light on enforcement mechanisms. Research ICT Africa's "Just AI" initiative builds a rights-based, African-grounded governance framework rather than importing Northern ones wholesale. On the ground, students operate under real law: POPIA (South Africa's data-protection act) and the AU's Malabo Convention — concrete answers to "where did your eval data come from, and may you use it?"

Then the sovereignty argument from Session 4 returns with teeth: if the levers (compute, chips, fabs, hyperscale data centres) sit largely in the North, what governance capacity does a country without them actually have? The 2025 Kigali "Africa Declaration on AI" and its much-cited $60bn fund are, as of 2026, a pledge with undefined governance — and one that still routes through Northern hardware. That gap between ambition and infrastructure is the honest subject of Session 18.

⚠️ Don't overclaim the politics

It is easy to cite the $60bn fund as if it were committed money or to treat the AU Strategy as binding regulation. Neither is true yet. Teaching the gap accurately — pledge vs capital, strategy vs enforcement — is more useful than the press-release version.

🧾 Summary & what's next

Technical AI governance is the engineering that decides whether a safety policy can be enforced. Reuel et al.'s capacities × targets grid maps the field; compute governance is the most tractable lever because it sits on a countable, detectable substrate; the three lab frameworks share a skeleton but are voluntary; agent governance is new and unsettled; and from Africa the available levers, the applicable law (POPIA, Malabo), and the sovereignty gap reshape the whole question.
