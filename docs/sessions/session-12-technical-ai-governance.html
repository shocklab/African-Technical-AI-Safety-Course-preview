Week 6 • Session 12

Technical AI governance

Assessment, access, verification, security, and what they look like from Africa

What we'll cover

Session 11 built evaluations: ways to measure what a model can do and whether it will. This session asks the next question. Given a measurement, who acts on it, and how would anyone verify compliance? That is technical AI governance: the engineering substrate that determines whether a governance proposal is enforceable or merely aspirational.

We use a map of the field, locate compute governance on it (callback to Session 4), compare three frontier-safety frameworks in depth, touch agent governance, and then look at the question from an African vantage point, where the RIA Just AI framework, the AU strategy, POPIA, and the sovereignty debate change which levers are even available.

A map: capacities × targets

Reuel et al.'s "Open Problems in Technical AI Governance" gives the field a two-dimensional taxonomy. Almost every concrete proposal is a cell in this grid.

The taxonomy

Capacity (what you do)What it means
AssessmentMeasuring capabilities, risks, and impacts (evals, audits, red-teaming).
AccessStructured, staged access for researchers/auditors without handing over everything.
VerificationConfirming claims are true: that a lab trained what it says, within agreed limits.
SecurityProtecting model weights and infrastructure from theft or tampering.
OperationalisationTurning fuzzy benchmarks into thresholds a regulator can use.
Ecosystem monitoringTracking how the AI field is evolving (compute flows, incidents, diffusion) and its real-world impacts.

Each capacity is applied across four targets along the value chain: data · compute · models/algorithms · deployment. "Compute verification via on-chip mechanisms" is one cell; "deployment assessment via evals" is another. The grid is a good way to locate and scope a governance project.

Compute governance

Back to the substrate

Recall Session 4: capability ← compute ← chips + energy. Compute is excludable, quantifiable, and detectable in a way data and algorithms are not. That is why it anchors the most concrete governance proposals: training-compute thresholds, export controls on advanced chips and fabrication equipment, and research into on-chip mechanisms that could make a chip's use verifiable.

It is also the lever with the sharpest equity edge: controlling compute controls who can build frontier systems at all, which plays out very differently depending on where you sit.

The thresholds are already concrete. The EU AI Act sets its systemic-risk line for general-purpose models at \(10^{25}\) training FLOP, and the 2023 US executive order (since rescinded) had used \(10^{26}\); both reach for compute because it is the one input a regulator can count, and in principle verify. Whether a fixed FLOP line is well-chosen is debated, since it ages as algorithms grow more efficient (2.3); but the choice itself shows why compute is the substrate governance reaches for first.

The three frontier-safety frameworks

The leading labs publish frontier-safety policies that share a common skeleton: capability thresholds → eval commitments → required mitigations → a commitment to pause if mitigations aren't ready.

Anthropic — RSP

The Responsible Scaling Policy defines AI Safety Levels (ASL); crossing a capability threshold triggers specified security and deployment safeguards.

OpenAI — Preparedness

Tracks frontier capabilities (bio/chem, cybersecurity, AI self-improvement) against High and Critical capability thresholds; crossing High gates deployment, Critical gates further development; findings are recorded in Capabilities Reports.

DeepMind — Frontier Safety Framework

Defines Critical Capability Levels and the mitigations expected as models approach them; v3 (2025) adds a harmful-manipulation CCL, misalignment risks such as shutdown resistance, and Tracked Capability Levels that flag less extreme risks earlier.

Under the surface all three share an if–then logic: if a model crosses a defined capability threshold on a specified evaluation, then a named set of mitigations (hardened security, deployment limits, or a training pause) becomes mandatory before work continues. The strength is that this commits a lab in advance, when the incentives are cleaner. These began as purely voluntary commitments; publishing one no longer is. California's SB 53 (signed September 2025) requires large frontier developers (annual revenue above $500m and training runs above \(10^{26}\) FLOP) to publish a frontier AI framework, to publish a justified update within 30 days of materially changing it, and to report critical safety incidents to the state's Office of Emergency Services within 15 days; the EU AI Act's obligations on general-purpose model providers have applied since August 2025. What the law has not changed is who does the grading: the lab runs its own eval, decides whether the threshold was crossed, and no external party checks either. That is the Verification gap in Reuel's grid (nothing external confirms the claim), and closing it with independent evals, audit access and hardware attestation is the live frontier.

Comparing the frameworks

METR's "Common Elements of Frontier AI Safety Policies" distils what these share and where they differ. The instructive class exercise (Session 12 seminar) is a jigsaw. Groups take one framework each, then cross-compare against the "Common Elements": what's shared, what's missing, and what's enforceable versus self-reported? For the largest developers publishing such a framework is now the law; grading it is still internal, which is itself the governance gap.

From an eval to a commitment

Make the if–then chain tangible. Suppose a dangerous-capability evaluation (Session 11) tests whether a model can materially help a non-expert plan a biological or large-scale cyber attack. The framework sets a threshold: score above it and the model is deemed to have crossed into a higher risk tier. The then follows automatically: the weights must sit behind hardened security (so an actor who could misuse them cannot steal them), deployment is gated behind specific safeguards (refusal training, monitoring, restricted access), and if those mitigations are not yet ready, training or release pauses until they are. The chain has run for real once: in May 2025 Anthropic activated ASL-3 protections for Claude Opus 4 as a precaution, not having determined that the model crossed the CBRN capability threshold but being unable to clearly rule it out. Every link in that chain is a technical artefact someone has to build, and someone ought to be able to verify. That is the subject of this session.

Agent governance

New surface area

Agentic systems take actions, use tools, and run for long horizons (11.2's scaffolded-agent evals; the untrusted agent of Session 10's control setting). Governance then has to cover actions, not only models: identifiers for AI agents, logging and rollback, liability for autonomous actions, and limits on what an agent may do without a human in the loop. This is among the least-settled corners of the field and a rich source of conceptual projects.

Where the technical contribution is

This is the session where a maths-and-code background pays off most directly: most cells in Reuel's grid are unsolved engineering problems, not policy ones.

Technical artefacts for governance

  • Dangerous-capability evaluations (assessment): the measurements a threshold keys on; only as trustworthy as their construct validity (Session 11).
  • Compute accounting and on-chip verification (verification, compute): mechanisms letting a third party confirm how much compute a chip used, or attest that a given model was or was not trained, without exposing the weights.
  • Structured access (access): APIs and staged-disclosure protocols that let auditors probe a model without receiving it outright.
  • Proof-of-training (verification, models): cryptographic or statistical evidence that a training run matched its declared recipe.
  • Safety cases (operationalisation): a structured, auditable argument that a system is safe enough to deploy, borrowed from aviation and nuclear engineering.

None of these is close to solved: technical AI governance is where safety research meets buildable tools, and a natural home for the kind of project this course ends in.

Governance from Africa

The taxonomy is universal; the available levers are not. From a South African vantage point the picture shifts.

Frameworks, law, and sovereignty

The AU Continental AI Strategy (2024) lists "minimise risks" among its focus areas and explicitly flags data-centre water use; it is a continental framework, though light on enforcement mechanisms. Research ICT Africa's "Just AI" initiative builds a rights-based, African-grounded governance framework rather than importing Northern ones wholesale. On the ground, students operate under real law. POPIA (South Africa's data-protection act) and the AU's Malabo Convention are concrete answers to "where did your eval data come from, and may you use it?"

Then the sovereignty argument from Session 4 returns with teeth: if the levers (compute, chips, fabs, hyperscale data centres) sit largely in the North, what governance capacity does a country without them have? The 2025 Kigali "Africa Declaration on AI" and its much-cited $60bn fund are, as of 2026, a pledge with undefined governance, and one that still routes through Northern hardware. That gap between ambition and infrastructure is the subject of Session 18.

Don't overclaim the politics

It is easy to cite the $60bn fund as if it were committed money or to treat the AU Strategy as binding regulation. Neither is true yet. Teaching the gap accurately (pledge vs capital, strategy vs enforcement) is more useful than the press-release version.

Questions to bring to class

  • The EU AI Act's systemic-risk line sits at \(10^{25}\) training FLOP. Algorithmic efficiency keeps lowering the compute needed for a given capability (2.3). Does a fixed FLOP threshold survive that, and if not, what should a regulator index the line to instead?
  • What does SB 53 change about the frontier-safety frameworks, and what does it leave untouched? Put the answer in Reuel's grid: which capacity did the law strengthen, and which gap remains open?
  • For the jigsaw: as you read your group's framework, mark each commitment as externally verifiable or self-reported. Pick one commitment from the second column and say what technical artefact would move it to the first.
  • From a South African vantage point, which of the six capacities in Reuel's grid could a country without frontier compute actually exercise, and on which targets?

Summary & what's next

Technical AI governance is the engineering that decides whether a safety policy can be enforced. Reuel et al.'s capacities × targets grid maps the field; compute governance is the most tractable lever because it sits on a countable, detectable substrate; the lab frameworks share a skeleton and remain self-graded, though the largest developers are now legally required to publish them; agent governance is new and unsettled; and from Africa the available levers, the applicable law (POPIA, Malabo), and the sovereignty gap reshape the question.
