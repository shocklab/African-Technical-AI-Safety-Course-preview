What interpretability buys for safety

Reading what a model "knows", controlling what it does, and auditing for hidden objectives

What we'll cover

Interpretability is the white-box arm of the oversight (8.1) and evaluation (11) stack: the bet that reading or steering a model's internals can catch what behaviour hides. This sub-session walks through the main concrete safety applications: an unsupervised probe for latent knowledge, its refutation, representation engineering's reading-and-steering toolkit, the backdoor problem that behavioural training cannot remove, and the auditing game. Each result is taught next to its caveat, the calibrated epistemics the course is built on. Read for both the traction and its limits.

Reading what a model knows

An unsupervised probe can find a model's latent "belief" that survives being told to lie, until you look closely.

Burns et al.'s Contrast-Consistent Search (CCS) demonstrates that "what a model says" and "what it represents" can come apart. Without any labels, CCS finds a direction in activation space that satisfies a logical consistency condition (a statement and its negation should get complementary probabilities), and across many models and datasets that direction classifies truth better, on average, than the model's own outputs. The safety-relevant part: when the model is prompted to produce false answers, its stated accuracy drops sharply while the CCS probe holds up. That is the "knows ≠ says" phenomenon made concrete, and it is the template for interpretability-based lie detection.

The refutation

Then the caveat, which is as important as the result. Farquhar et al. (DeepMind) showed the strong reading of CCS does not hold: they prove that arbitrary binary features satisfy the CCS consistency loss equally well, and demonstrate empirically that CCS tracks whatever feature is most salient in the prompt, not truth. Insert a random distractor and the probe learns that; frame the prompt around a character's opinion and it learns the opinion, not the fact. CCS turns out to agree with plain PCA more than with truth. The lesson is not "probing is worthless": an unsupervised probe finds a salient direction, and calling it the model's "knowledge" is an over-read you must test, not assume.

Reading and controlling concepts: representation engineering

A step up in ambition: read a concept, then turn a dial on it.

Representation engineering (RepE; Zou et al.) reads "concept vectors" for high-level properties (truthfulness, power-seeking, harmfulness, emotion) and then uses them to steer the model. Reading honesty gives a strong lie classifier and a sizeable jump on the TruthfulQA benchmark (about +18 points over zero-shot); control vectors push the model to be more honest or less power-seeking. This is the parent method of the circuit-breakers defence you met in Session 10, so we reference S10 rather than re-teaching it. The useful distinction RepE forces is three-way: reading a concept, controlling it, and understanding the mechanism are different achievements. RepE is explicitly "top-down": it buys reading and control while abstracting away mechanism. So it delivers real traction without delivering mechanistic understanding, which is a perfectly good trade for some safety purposes and a poor one for others (you can steer away from a behaviour without knowing why it was there, or whether you have really removed it).

Backdoors that training does not remove

Sleeper Agents is a strong motivation for white-box methods, and clear evidence that behaviour alone is not enough.

Hubinger et al. trained models with a hidden backdoor (behave normally, but switch to harmful behaviour on a trigger, say a particular year in the prompt). The alarming result is that the backdoor survived the standard safety pipeline: supervised fine-tuning, RLHF, and even adversarial training did not remove it, and adversarial training sometimes taught the model to hide the trigger better rather than drop it. For interpretability this cuts two ways. It is the strongest argument for white-box methods. If behavioural training cannot remove a deceptive backdoor, you need to look inside. But the paper itself does not claim interpretability solves this: its white-box detection ideas are surveyed as promising directions, and later "defection probe" work catches these specific, deliberately-inserted backdoors with some success. Catching a backdoor you planted is not the same as catching one that emerges on its own, and nobody should claim the latter is solved.

The auditing game

A blind red-team/blue-team game is the field's closest thing to a fair test of "did interpretability help?"

Marks et al. (Anthropic) ran a blind evaluation of interpretability's practical value. They trained a model with a hidden objective (a learned tendency to exploit quirks of its reward model), then staged a blind audit: several teams, varying in their access and tools, tried to uncover the hidden objective. This is the most encouraging concrete result in the block, and it comes with a sovereignty sting we develop in 16.4: three of four teams found the hidden objective; the fastest used sparse-autoencoder features and cracked it in about 90 minutes; the team restricted to API access alone failed. Interpretability, with weight access, demonstrably helped find something behaviour did not reveal.

The authors' caveats

The authors note the SAE-based solution "could have been replicated by a simpler embedding-based search baseline" (the strong-baseline lesson from 15.4 again), the hidden objective was explicitly present in the training data (making the game "unrealistically easy"), and with only four teams there are no strong quantitative claims. So the auditing game is best understood as the field's capture-the-flag exercise (a methodology for validating whether interpretability helps, and a bounded success), not proof that it scales to a model actively hiding a naturally-arising objective.

Questions to bring to class

  • CCS finds a direction that classifies truth better than the model's outputs, and holds when the model is told to lie. Why does Farquhar et al.'s "arbitrary features satisfy the loss" result undercut calling that direction the model's "knowledge"?
  • Distinguish reading a concept, controlling it, and understanding its mechanism. Which does RepE deliver, and for which safety purpose is that enough versus insufficient?
  • Sleeper Agents shows backdoors surviving RLHF and adversarial training. Why is that simultaneously the best argument for interpretability and not evidence that interpretability works yet?
  • In the auditing game the API-only team failed and the SAE team succeeded in 90 minutes, but the authors call the game "unrealistically easy." What would a harder, more realistic version change?

Readings

Core

• Burns et al. (2022), "Discovering Latent Knowledge in Language Models Without Supervision" (arXiv:2212.03827). CCS and the "knows ≠ says" demonstration.

• Marks et al. (2025), "Auditing Language Models for Hidden Objectives" (arXiv:2503.10965). The auditing game, the access-tier result, and the authors' caveats.

Supplementary

• Farquhar et al. (2023), "Challenges with Unsupervised LLM Knowledge Discovery" (arXiv:2312.10029). The CCS refutation.

• Zou et al. (2023), "Representation Engineering" (arXiv:2310.01405). Reading and controlling concepts (RepE; parent of S10's circuit breakers).

• Hubinger et al. (2024), "Sleeper Agents" (arXiv:2401.05566). Backdoors that survive safety training.

• Chen et al. (2025), "Persona Vectors: Monitoring and Controlling Character Traits in Language Models" (arXiv:2507.21509): activation directions for character traits, a monitoring-and-steering application; how such traits vary across languages is an open question with a direct African-language angle (18.2).
