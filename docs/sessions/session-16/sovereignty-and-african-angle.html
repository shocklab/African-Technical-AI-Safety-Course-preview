Interpretability and sovereignty

The African angle (auditing a model you did not train), the English-centrism of current tools, and where the angle is thin

What we'll cover

This closes the interpretability block and the taught core of the course. We have been candid that interpretability is where the African lens is thinnest, and we hold to the course's own rule: do not manufacture an angle. There is one strong angle here, backed by a verified result: interpretability is the technical precondition for auditing a model you did not train. There are two more that are real but double-edged: the English-centrism of current tools, and the compute cost of interpreting at all. And there is an honest gap, which we name as a gap and turn into a research prompt rather than papering over.

Auditing a model you did not train

Access tier decides whether you can audit at all. That is a sovereignty fact, not a metaphor.

African institutions overwhelmingly receive frontier models (as weights, or more often as an API) rather than building them. For such an actor, the developer's safety claims are unverifiable from the outside, and behavioural testing is shallow (Sessions 10–11). Interpretability is the one safety tool that operates on the artefact itself, needing neither the training data nor the developer's cooperation. The auditing game of 16.2 turns this from rhetoric into a measured result: the teams that found the model's hidden objective had weight access; the team restricted to API access alone failed. Access tier determined whether the audit was even possible. That is the sovereignty argument with empirical teeth, and it links straight to two earlier sessions: the "who holds the keys" point of Session 10 (a control protocol needs the stack owner) and the audit/access governance of Session 12 (a "right to inspect" is hollow unless tools exist that work on weights, and unless you have the weights to point them at). For a government, hospital network, or university deploying an imported model, "can we audit what we run?" reduces to "do we have the weights, the skills, and the compute?", and interpretability is the skill half of that triple.

English-centrism and the isiZulu gap

Current interpretability is validated on English; the safety gaps this course cares about might be invisible to it.

The course's anchor is that frontier models are measurably less safe in isiZulu (Sessions 1, 9). Ask the interpretability question: would today's tools even see that gap? The evidence says: not reliably, and not yet. Interpretability findings (probes, SAE features) are trained and validated on English-dominant data, and Session 15.4 gave a concrete warning: a sparse-autoencoder "living room" latent fired on the English phrase but was nearly dead on its French translation. The feature was bound to the surface language, not the concept. If many features are like that, then a "refusal" or "harmfulness" direction discovered on English need not exist, or fire, for an isiZulu prompt, and you cannot read a concept you have not elicited, which needs in-language data. A second, capability-confounded datapoint: the circuit-breakers multilingual results show frontier models behave erratically and differently across languages, not simply worse in low-resource ones, and current interpretability gives us almost no principled window into why. So the isiZulu safety gap is not just unmeasured by interpretability; with off-the-shelf tools it may be close to invisible. That is a real, testable research gap, not a rhetorical flourish.

The compute cost of interpreting

Interpreting a frontier model is itself a frontier-scale activity, with one liberating exception.

The asymmetry from earlier sessions returns. Training the wide sparse autoencoders that interpret a production model is expensive: the auditing-game SAE was around a million features wide on a production model's residual stream, and circuit analysis on a 70B model (16.3) is not laptop work. The institutions that can train frontier models can interpret them; those that receive them often cannot, which loops back to the access argument. The liberating exception: interpreting a given set of activations is far cheaper than pretraining. A small SAE trains in about a GPU-hour, and pretrained SAEs and tools (SAELens) are distributed freely, so interpretation of open models is accessible. The binding constraint is access to frontier weights, not to interpretation compute. That tension is the interesting version of the compute angle.

Where this angle is thin

Say it plainly, because the course promised to. This is the thinnest African angle in the course. There is no isiZulu sparse autoencoder, no African-language interpretability dataset, no body of work interpreting models in African languages that we could point you to. The foundational mathematics of Sessions 13 and 15 (QK/OV, superposition, the SAE objective) is geography- and language-neutral, and we are not going to pretend otherwise. What is real is the sovereignty argument (strong), the English-centrism question (open), and the compute asymmetry (structural). The rest is a gap. We flag it as a gap rather than inflate it, because a manufactured angle would betray the critical-reading habit this course is trying to build.

An honours-project brief (optional)

The gap is generative, and it is tractable on a small open model. A concrete project: does a "refusal" or "harmfulness" direction exist in an isiZulu-prompted open-weights model, and can you read it? Build a small probing set in isiZulu and English; train linear probes (and, if you can, look at a pretrained SAE's features) on the activations of an open model under matched English and isiZulu prompts; test whether the English-derived direction transfers, fires, or is simply absent in-language. Whatever you find is a contribution: the frontier-lab interpretability literature has not run this. It ties the block back to the course's anchor (Sessions 1, 9), to evaluation (Session 11), and to the Global-South session of the project phase (Session 18). The thin angle, turned into your work.

Questions to bring to class

  • Why does the auditing-game result (API-only team failed, weight teams succeeded) make "access tier" a sovereignty question rather than a convenience? Link it to Sessions 10 and 12.
  • The "living room" SAE latent fired in English but not French. Spell out why that threatens the visibility of the isiZulu safety gap to current interpretability tools.
  • Interpreting open models is cheap; the constraint is weight access. Why does that make interpretability a governance/access problem more than a compute problem for African deployers?
  • Design the smallest honest experiment that would tell you whether a "harmfulness" direction transfers from English to isiZulu in an open model. What result would mean the gap is invisible to interpretability?
