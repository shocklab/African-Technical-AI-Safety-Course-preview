Week 8 • Session 16.3

The limits of interpretability

Interpretability illusions, the scalability gap, and why "we found a feature" is not yet "we have a safety guarantee"

What we'll cover

This sub-session carries equal weight to 16.2: a safety tool you over-trust is more dangerous than one you distrust. Interpretability has real wins (16.2), and a sober counterweight: explanations that look clean but mean the wrong thing (illusions), a gap between finding circuits and understanding them at scale, a field-level reassessment of how much has actually been delivered, and the hard fact that none of it yet yields a safety guarantee. One phrase from the field's own state of play names the real risk: false assurance.

Interpretability illusions

A clean-looking explanation can be confidently wrong, and there are now named, demonstrated ways it happens.

The first illusion is about features. Bolukbasi et al. found that a BERT neuron whose top-activating examples look like a single tidy concept (say, "song titles") encodes a completely different concept on a different dataset (historical events, novel excerpts). The "interpretation" was an artefact of which slice of data you looked through. Different datasets occupy different regions of activation space, each locally coherent, and the human over-reads. The fix is a discipline: always test an interpretation on multiple datasets before believing it.

The patching illusion

The second illusion strikes at the causal tools of 15.1. Makelov, Nanda and colleagues showed that a subspace activation patch can change the output as if you had flipped a meaningful feature, while actually working through a dormant, causally-disconnected pathway: it exploits a direction in the kernel of a downstream weight matrix, so the intervention "succeeds" without touching the mechanism you thought you were probing. Worse, they argue such subspaces are probably common (most MLPs between two communicating components contain one). This is not nihilism: they give sanity checks (decompose the patched direction into the part that lies in the active row-space versus the null-space, and verify which one drives the effect) and a clean success case. The lesson is the recurring one. A positive interpretability result needs validation, because the method can manufacture a convincing wrong answer.

Circuit analysis at scale

The mechanics of circuit analysis reach frontier models. The understanding does not, yet.

Lieberum, Nanda and colleagues asked in 2023 whether circuit analysis survives at frontier scale, studying multiple-choice behaviour in the 70B-parameter Chinchilla model. The two-sided answer is worth teaching. The tooling scales: logit attribution, attention visualisation, and activation patching all work on a 70B model, and they find clean "correct-letter heads." But the semantic understanding is "mixed": their best account of what those heads encode (something like "the Nth item in an enumeration") only partly explains the behaviour on broader distributions. So we can locate circuits at scale; we cannot yet fully understand them at scale, which is the superposition/labelling difficulty of Session 13.2 reappearing where it matters most.

The 2025 attribution-graph work (15.4) moves the frontier of the same story: circuit tracing on Claude 3.5 Haiku, a production model, reading out multi-step reasoning, poem planning, and multilingual circuits from single prompts. The two-sided verdict survives the update. The graphs are built one prompt at a time; the transcoder replacement model matches the underlying model's next-token behaviour on only about half of a diverse prompt set, with "error nodes" absorbing what it misses; and attention patterns are taken as given rather than explained, which leaves whole classes of behaviour (including induction) outside the method's reach. Between 2023 and 2025 the tooling went from a 70B research model to a deployed frontier model; a complete mechanistic account of either still does not exist.

The field's own assessment

A 2025 multi-lab review names the open problems plainly, and there are many.

The most credible current assessment is the 2025 "Open Problems in Mechanistic Interpretability" review, written by roughly thirty authors across Anthropic, DeepMind, Apollo, academia and beyond. Its central admissions: sparse dictionary learning lacks solid theoretical foundations, and the features an SAE finds depend on the training dataset (an SAE trained on pretraining data generally lacks a good "refusing harmful requests" feature; one trained on chat data has one, an awkward property for something billed as a principled decomposition). It is open whether the superposition hypothesis underpinning the whole SAE programme is fundamentally valid or merely a useful approximation. There has been "limited work testing whether SAEs are practically useful in downstream applications" (15.4). And on the dream of provable safety: formal verification of real systems is well beyond current capabilities, and "enumerative safety" (find all the concerning features) is listed as an open question. Pair this with Nanda's own measured "theories of impact" survey, which lists many routes interpretability could matter while flagging the catches: it only helps if labs act on audits (coordination), a deceptive model could think in unfamiliar concepts to evade reading (deception evasion), interpretability used in the training loop gets optimised against (Goodharting), and better interpretability can accelerate capabilities.

"Streetlight interpretability"

Put the scalability gap and the dataset-dependence together and you get the streetlight critique: like the drunk searching for keys under the lamppost because that is where the light is, the field tends to interpret what is easy to interpret (clean algorithmic tasks, the concepts our datasets happen to illuminate) rather than what matters for safety. This is a named lens rather than a single paper's claim; the evidence is exactly the two results above: we explain the toy task cleanly and the broad distribution messily (Chinchilla), and we find the features our data lights up (the dataset-dependence point). It is a useful habit of suspicion.

The guarantee gap and false assurance

Even the wins are partial, and partial wins invite the most dangerous failure of all: false confidence.

Notice that every win in 16.2 came fenced. Circuit breakers leave a nonzero residual attack rate and cover only "generically harmful" content. The auditing-game success was on an "unrealistically easy" target and matchable by a non-interpretability baseline. CCS found a salient direction, not truth. None of these is a guarantee, a statement of the form "this model provably will not do X." The Open Problems review's conclusion: interpretability could aid governance and safety, "however, realizing these benefits will require careful attention to the risks of potential misuse and of giving false assurance about AI safety." That is the hazard for this block. An interpretability result that looks like a safety guarantee, accepted as one by a regulator or a deploying institution, is worse than no result, because it licenses deployment on a foundation that has not actually been laid. The honest posture is the calibrated one the whole course has practised: interpretability is the most exciting safety tool and the one most likely to be over-sold, and telling the difference is the skill.

Questions to bring to class

  • Describe the BERT and subspace-patching illusions. What single methodological discipline would have caught each?
  • "The mechanics scale but the understanding does not." What does the Chinchilla study find that scales, and what does not? How is that the Session 13.2 problem returning?
  • Why is the dataset-dependence of SAE features "an awkward property for a principled decomposition"? Give an example where it would mislead a safety audit.
  • Explain "false assurance" and why it can make a partial interpretability result worse than none. How would you word an honest interpretability claim to a regulator?

Readings

Core

Supplementary

• Bolukbasi et al. (2021), "An Interpretability Illusion for BERT" (arXiv:2104.07143).

• Makelov, Lange & Nanda (2023), "Is This the Subspace You Are Looking For?" (arXiv:2311.17030). The activation-patching illusion.

• Lieberum et al. (2023), "Does Circuit Analysis Interpretability Scale?" (arXiv:2307.09458). Circuit analysis in 70B Chinchilla.

• Nanda, "A Longlist of Theories of Impact for Interpretability" (Alignment Forum). The measured insider view, with caveats.
