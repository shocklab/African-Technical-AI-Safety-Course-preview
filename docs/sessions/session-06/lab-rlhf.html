Week 3 • Session 6.5

Lab — reward models & over-optimisation

Train a reward model from preferences, then watch Goodhart happen with your own model

What we'll cover

This lab makes Session 6 concrete and Colab-friendly. You'll train a small reward model on preference pairs and check it ranks held-out pairs correctly; then — the centrepiece — use best-of-\(n\) sampling as a cheap, RL-free stand-in for policy optimisation and watch over-optimisation happen: a proxy score that keeps rising while true quality peaks and falls (6.4). An optional GPU track runs a real RLHF step in ARENA.

Setup Colab — core tasks run on CPU

Open a Colab notebook and install:

!pip install "transformers>=4.44" datasets torch

For the reward model, use a small base such as distilbert-base-uncased or gpt2 with a scalar head; for preference data, take a small slice of an open set (e.g. the Anthropic HH-RLHF dataset on the Hugging Face Hub). Save the notebook to submit.

① Train a reward model

Bradley–Terry, in code

  • Load a few thousand preference pairs \((x, y_w, y_l)\). Build a model that maps a (prompt, response) to a scalar \(r_\phi(x,y)\) (a transformer with a linear reward head).
  • Train with the Bradley–Terry loss from 6.2: loss = -F.logsigmoid(r_w - r_l).mean() — i.e. \(\mathcal{L} = -\,\mathbb{E}[\log \sigma(r_\phi(x,y_w) - r_\phi(x,y_l))]\).
  • Evaluate on a held-out split: what fraction of pairs does it rank correctly (reward of the preferred response higher)? Report this preference accuracy — a well-trained RM on an easy split lands well above chance but rarely near 100%.

② Watch over-optimisation: best-of-n

Goodhart on your laptop

Best-of-\(n\) is the cheapest possible "optimiser": sample \(n\) responses from a base model, score them all with your reward model, and keep the highest-scoring one. As \(n\) grows you are optimising harder against the proxy — exactly what RL does, without the RL. The experiment:

  • For a set of prompts, generate \(n\) responses each for \(n = 1, 2, 4, 8, 16, \dots\) and select the top by your reward model. Plot the selected response's proxy reward against \(n\) (or against \(\sqrt{\ln n}\), the over-optimisation axis).
  • Now score the same selected responses with a different, held-out judge of "true" quality — a second reward model, a stronger model used as an LLM-judge, or your own ratings on a small sample. Plot this "gold" score on the same axis.
  • You should see the signature of Goodhart (6.4): the proxy score rises monotonically with \(n\), while the gold score rises then plateaus or falls. Identify the \(n\) at which they diverge — that is over-optimisation, made with your own hands.

③ Optional: a real RLHF step [GPU — optional]

ARENA Chapter 2

On a free Colab GPU, work through the RLHF notebook in ARENA (Chapter 2): a reward model plus a policy-gradient/PPO step on a small model and a toy objective, with the KL penalty (6.3) in place. Watch what happens to sample quality and to the KL as you train — and try setting the KL coefficient \(\beta\) to zero to see the policy run off into the reward model's blind spots.

African-safety probe

Whose preferences does the reward model encode?

Construct a handful of preference pairs in isiZulu (or another language you read) — a clearly-better and a clearly-worse response to the same prompt. Score them with an off-the-shelf reward model (or your trained one). Does it rank them sensibly, or does its accuracy collapse outside English? Note what you find: a reward model that can't judge a language has nothing useful to teach the policy there — a concrete instance of the evaluation-representativeness problem we take up in Session 11.

Session 6 & Week 3 summary — what's next

RLHF breaks SFT's ceiling by learning from comparisons (6.1): a Bradley–Terry reward model turns preferences into a scalar proxy (6.2), and the policy is optimised against it under a KL leash with PPO — or directly with DPO (6.3). The price is steep and now familiar: optimising a learned proxy invites reward hacking, over-optimisation and sycophancy, and human feedback cannot supervise superhuman behaviour (6.4); you've now seen all of this in code (6.5). With Week 3 done, Part II has its foundation: we know how an assistant is built, and exactly where its "alignment" is thin.

Next (Week 4, Session 7): the first response to RLHF's human-feedback ceiling — replace (some) human feedback with AI feedback: RLAIF and Constitutional AI. Then Session 8 confronts scalable oversight and the question of whose values.
