Does the eval measure what it claims?

Construct validity, borrowed from psychometrics, and the effect of metric choice

What we'll cover

Suppose you elicited perfectly and the model did not sandbag. There is still a prior question: does the eval measure the thing its name claims? A benchmark called "reasoning" might measure pattern-matching; "dangerous knowledge" might measure recall of facts that are not the bottleneck to harm. This is the question of validity, and the evaluation literature is belatedly importing it from a field that has wrestled with it for a century: psychometrics. We bring in the vocabulary, organise it around the "everything in the whole wide world" critique, and show with a worked example how the choice of metric alone can flip a smooth trend into an apparent emergent jump. Construct validity is the representativeness problem in disguise, and the African-lens thread becomes a measurement argument here.

Validity, borrowed from psychometrics

Measuring an abstract construct through a concrete test is the problem psychology has studied for a hundred years.

Psychometrics distinguishes several things that "is this a good test?" runs together, and the distinctions transfer cleanly to model evals.

The validity vocabulary

  • Content validity: do the items cover the construct? A "coding" benchmark of only string-manipulation problems has poor content validity for coding in general.
  • Construct validity: does the test measure the abstract thing it claims (the "construct": reasoning, dangerous capability, alignment) rather than some correlate? This is the hard one and the one most eval claims quietly assume.
  • Criterion validity: does the score predict the real-world outcome you care about? Does a high WMDP score actually predict uplift in causing harm?
  • Reliability: separate from validity. Does the test give the same answer on repeats? An eval whose score swings with the random seed is unreliable before it is even invalid.

A benchmark can be reliable and still invalid (it consistently measures the wrong thing), and the two failures need different fixes. Most heated "the model is/is not capable" disputes are really disputes about construct validity that neither side has named as such.

The "everything in the whole wide world" problem

A benchmark that claims to measure a general ability usually measures a narrow slice and over-claims the rest.

Raji, Bender and colleagues (2021) give the field's sharpest critique, named after a children's book in which Grover tours a museum claiming to showcase everything in the whole wide world; its rooms hold small assortments of particular things, and the tour ends at a door marked "Everything Else" that opens onto the world outside. Benchmarks marketed as measuring "general" language understanding or reasoning, they argue, rest on the same move: a finite, particular set of tasks is presented as a proxy for an open-ended human ability, and a high score is read as evidence of the general capacity. The paper's diagnosis is explicitly about construct validity (these benchmarks "cannot possibly capture anything representative" of the sweeping claims made about them), and it identifies three recurring limits: task design that is narrower than the claim, data stripped of its real-world context, and benchmarks used by communities for purposes their construction never justified. The lesson for safety is direct: when a model "passes a reasoning benchmark," ask what construct the benchmark actually samples before you let the word "reasoning" do any work.

The construct question, applied to WMDP

Make it concrete with a benchmark from Session 10. WMDP measures multiple-choice recall of hazardous knowledge: facts about pathogens, exploits, chemistry. The construct it is used to stand for is uplift: how much the model raises a malicious actor's chance of actually causing harm. Are those the same? Recall is plausibly necessary for uplift, but it is far from sufficient: building a weapon is gated by tacit skill, materials, and lab access that no multiple-choice question tests, and conversely a model that fails the quiz might still help by reasoning rather than recalling. So WMDP has reasonable content validity for "hazardous facts" and uncertain criterion validity for "uplift." None of this means WMDP is useless; it means a governance threshold written on WMDP is a threshold on recall, and the inference to real-world danger is a separate, contestable step that the score does not establish on its own.

Discontinuous metrics and the emergence mirage

The same model outputs can show a smooth trend or a sudden jump, depending only on how you score them.

Session 2.4 met this as the "emergence mirage," and it belongs here too as a demonstration that a measurement choice can manufacture a scientific claim. Schaeffer et al. (2023) showed that many "emergent abilities" (capabilities that appear to switch on abruptly at some scale) are artefacts of a discontinuous metric. Score a task with exact-match (all-or-nothing: the whole multi-token answer must be right) and performance looks flat then suddenly jumps. Score the identical model outputs with a continuous metric (per-token accuracy, or token edit distance) and the same data reveal smooth, gradual improvement. The model did not change; the ruler did. We do not re-derive the argument here (it is in 2.4), but its moral is a validity moral: the metric is part of the construct, and a poorly chosen metric can produce a finding ("emergence!") that better measurement dissolves.

Worked: exact-match versus a continuous metric

In the lab and in class, take one fixed set of model outputs on a multi-step task and score it two ways. Under exact-match, a 5-step answer is correct only if all 5 steps are right; if per-step accuracy is \(p\), exact-match accuracy is \(p^5\), which stays near zero until \(p\) climbs high and then shoots up, an "emergent" curve. Under a continuous metric (fraction of steps correct), the score is just \(p\), a straight, gradual line through the same runs. Plot both from the same data and the contrast is stark: one licenses the claim "the ability emerged at scale," the other licenses "the ability improved smoothly." A student who can produce both plots from one set of outputs has internalised a central habit of eval-reading: ask what the metric is doing before you believe what the curve is saying.

Construct validity and English-only benchmarks

Now the African-lens thread becomes a measurement argument. A benchmark built from English text, scored as "language understanding," and used to certify a model as broadly capable or broadly safe, is making the everything-in-the-whole-wide-world move: a narrow, English slice presented as the general construct. That is a construct-validity failure, and it is the same failure that makes an English-only safety eval miss the isiZulu jailbreak of Session 1. So the demand for in-language evaluation is not a separate diversity requirement layered on top of the science; it is the validity requirement the science already insists on. "We measured safety" is only true for the distribution sampled, and a benchmark that samples English and claims to measure safety has overstated its construct in the way Raji and Bender describe.

Questions to bring to class

  • Distinguish reliability from validity with an example of an eval that is reliable but invalid. Which is easier to detect, and why?
  • WMDP has good content validity for "hazardous facts." What evidence would you need to establish its criterion validity for "uplift to cause harm"? Why is that evidence hard to get?
  • Show that exact-match accuracy on a 5-step task is \(p^5\) for per-step accuracy \(p\). At what \(p\) does \(p^5\) start to rise steeply, and how does that produce an "emergent" curve?
  • Explain, in construct-validity terms, why an English-only safety benchmark over-claims when it is reported as measuring "safety." How is this the same error as the museum that claims to showcase everything in the whole wide world?
