Why evaluate: the eval taxonomy

What kind of claim an evaluation can support: capability versus propensity, behavioural versus internal

What we'll cover

Sessions 6 through 10 kept quoting numbers: a reward model's accuracy, a jailbreak's success rate, a control protocol's safety, an unlearning method's drop on WMDP. This session asks the question underneath all of them: how do you know the number is real? Evaluation is the empirical backbone of every safety claim, and it is also where a confident-looking measurement can quietly be wrong. We begin with the founding distinction (does the model have a dangerous capability, versus does it have the propensity to use one) and a small taxonomy that organises the rest of the session. An eval is evidence about the specific distribution it samples, and almost every frontier eval samples English.

Evaluation as the basis of safety claims

Every governance proposal and every safety case rests on a measurement someone trusts.

Session 12 will describe frontier-safety frameworks whose whole structure is "if a model crosses a capability threshold on a specified evaluation, then a set of mitigations becomes mandatory." That if–then is only as good as the eval in the "if." A responsible scaling policy that triggers on a bad measurement either cries wolf or, worse, certifies a dangerous model as safe. So evaluation is the instrument the entire governance edifice reads off. Shevlane et al. (2023), in the paper that organised the field, put the status plainly: model evaluation is necessary but not sufficient for safety. Necessary, because you cannot govern what you cannot measure; insufficient, because a measurement can be incomplete, gamed, or simply invalid, in ways the next three sub-sessions dissect.

Capability versus propensity

Two different questions, needing two different kinds of eval.

Shevlane et al. separate two things that everyday talk of "is the model safe?" runs together.

Dangerous-capability versus alignment evaluations

  • Dangerous-capability evaluation: does the model have a capability that could cause extreme harm? Can it find a software exploit, design a toxin, autonomously acquire resources? This asks about the ceiling of what the model can do if it tries.
  • Alignment (propensity) evaluation: setting aside what it can do, does it have the propensity to apply a capability for harm? Would it, in deployment, actually pursue a misaligned goal or resist correction?

The two come apart in both dangerous directions. A model can be capable but well-behaved (high capability, low harmful propensity), which is the hoped-for case. Or it can be capable and willing, which is the feared case. There is also a subtler case: a model can appear incapable or harmless on the eval while being neither, because it was poorly elicited or because it strategically underperformed. Capability evals try to establish an upper bound on danger; propensity evals try to establish what the model is inclined to do with that capability.

The taxonomy

Four cross-cutting distinctions tell you what an eval result actually licenses you to say.

Beyond capability-versus-propensity, three more axes recur, and naming them prevents a common error: reading an eval as proving more than it does.

Three more axes

  • Behavioural (black-box) versus internal (white-box). A behavioural eval reads only inputs and outputs; an internal eval reads activations or weights (a deception probe, a logit-lens read). Session 10.4 already showed why this matters: a black-box unlearning eval certified knowledge as gone that a white-box probe found still present. Behavioural access alone is, in Casper et al.'s phrase, insufficient for many safety claims.
  • Static benchmark versus agentic / long-horizon. A static benchmark scores single question-answer pairs; an agentic eval watches a model use tools and act over a long task. Dangerous capabilities (autonomy, self-proliferation) only show up in the agentic setting.
  • Upper bound versus lower bound. This is the one most often confused. A capability eval that elicits hard estimates an upper bound on what the model can do; a casual prompt estimates a lower bound. Reporting a lower bound as if it were the ceiling is how a model gets wrongly cleared, the theme of 11.2.

The 2×2 grid

Cross capability-versus-propensity with behavioural-versus-internal and you get four cells, and "the model passed the eval" means something different in each. Place these and the differences become obvious: WMDP (behavioural capability), an agentic cyber-range exercise (behavioural capability, long-horizon), Apollo's in-context scheming suite (behavioural propensity), a linear deception probe (internal propensity), and an isiZulu refusal eval (behavioural propensity, in a non-English distribution). A "pass" in the behavioural-propensity cell ("it did not scheme when we watched") is a far weaker safety claim than a pass in the internal-capability cell, and the taxonomy is what stops you from quietly upgrading one to the other.

Evaluators and the theory of change

Know the ecosystem by name, and where the eval result is supposed to go.

A handful of organisations define the practice, and they map onto the taxonomy. METR and the UK AI Security Institute (renamed from AI Safety Institute in 2025) focus on capability and agentic evals (METR's autonomy task suites; the Institute's open-source Inspect framework, now a de-facto standard). Apollo Research specialises in propensity and deception evals. The labs run their own: Anthropic's Responsible Scaling Policy and Google DeepMind's Frontier Safety Framework both gate decisions on capability thresholds. The intended path is a chain: an eval produces a measurement, the measurement feeds a risk assessment, the risk assessment triggers a governance action. That chain, eval to threshold to mitigation, is the subject of Session 12, so here we state it and hand it over. This session covers the first link only: making the measurement trustworthy.

The distribution an eval samples

An evaluation is evidence about one thing: the distribution of inputs it actually sampled. A summarisation benchmark tells you about summarisation; a multiple-choice bio test tells you about that question style; an English red-team tells you about English. The moment you generalise "scored safe on the eval" to "is safe," you have made a claim about a wider distribution than you measured, and almost every frontier safety eval samples an English-only distribution. The African-lens thread of this session is what you get when you take the validity question seriously and ask which distribution was sampled. It runs through 11.3 (where it becomes construct validity) and 11.5 (where you build the missing in-language eval yourself).

Questions to bring to class

  • Give a concrete model that would score well on a propensity eval but badly on a capability eval, and one that would do the reverse. What does each result tell a deployer?
  • Why is a behavioural "it did not misbehave when observed" a weaker safety claim than an internal probe showing it has no misaligned representation? Link this to the Session 10.4 black-box-evaluation failure.
  • Place a dangerous-capability cyber eval, an isiZulu refusal eval, and a deception probe in the capability/propensity × behavioural/internal grid. Which cell is hardest to evaluate well, and why?
  • "Necessary but not sufficient." Give one reason evaluation is necessary for safety governance and one concrete way a passed eval can still leave a model unsafe.

Readings

Core

• Shevlane et al. (2023), "Model evaluation for extreme risks" (arXiv:2305.15324): the capability/propensity split and the "necessary but not sufficient" framing.

Supplementary

• Casper et al. (2024), "Black-Box Access is Insufficient for Rigorous AI Audits" (arXiv:2401.14446): the behavioural-versus-internal access taxonomy.
