The statistics of evals

Treat an evaluation as an experiment: error bars on pass rates, paired comparisons, and time-horizon curves

What we'll cover

An eval score is a statistic computed from a finite sample of questions, so it has sampling error, yet leaderboards routinely report it to the decimal point with no interval. This sub-session, built on Evan Miller's Adding Error Bars to Evals, treats an eval as the experiment it is: a pass rate is a sample proportion with a confidence interval; two models on shared questions should be compared with a paired test; and you should know your statistical power before you run. We work the arithmetic by hand, then read METR's time-horizon result as an integrative case. A one-to-two-point leaderboard gap on a few hundred questions is usually not significant, and saying so is the difference between a measurement and a vibe.

A pass rate is a sample proportion

The eval questions are a sample; the score is an estimate; estimates need intervals.

Think of the benchmark's questions as a sample drawn from a large "super-population" of questions you could have asked. The model's true ability is the pass rate over that whole population; your measured score is an estimate of it from \(n\) questions. For \(k\) passes out of \(n\), the estimate and its standard error are

\[ \hat{p} = \frac{k}{n}, \qquad \mathrm{SE}(\hat{p}) = \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}}, \qquad \text{95\% CI} \approx \hat{p} \pm 1.96\,\mathrm{SE}. \]

Worked: 43 out of 50

A model passes 43 of 50 questions: \(\hat{p} = 0.86\). Then \(\mathrm{SE} = \sqrt{0.86 \times 0.14 / 50} = \sqrt{0.00241} \approx 0.049\), and the 95% interval is \(0.86 \pm 1.96(0.049) = 0.86 \pm 0.096\), roughly \([0.76,\,0.96]\). That is a wide interval (nearly twenty points) on a 50-question eval, which is sobering if you were about to celebrate beating a rival at 0.78. (Near the 0 or 1 boundary the symmetric normal interval misbehaves; the Wilson interval is the standard fix and you should prefer it for extreme rates. The qualitative lesson is unchanged: small \(n\) means fat error bars.)

Clustered errors and paired comparisons

Questions cluster, and models share questions; ignoring either gives wrong intervals.

Clustered standard errors

The formula above assumes independent questions. Real benchmarks group them (ten questions about one passage, twenty variants of one template) and answers within a cluster are correlated. Treating \(n\) correlated questions as \(n\) independent ones understates the standard error, sometimes badly: Miller notes the corrected (clustered) SE can be more than three times the naïve one. The effective \(n\) is closer to the number of independent clusters than the number of questions, and an interval computed without clustering can be three times too narrow, turning noise into a "significant" result.

Paired comparisons between models

To compare two models, do not put a separate independent CI on each and check for overlap; that throws away the fact that they answered the same questions. Compare them paired, question by question. If each model has per-question variance \(v\) and their scores correlate at \(\rho\), the variance of the difference is

\[ \mathrm{Var}(\hat{p}_A - \hat{p}_B) = v_A + v_B - 2\rho\sqrt{v_A v_B} \;\approx\; 2v(1-\rho). \]

At \(\rho = 0.5\) the paired standard error is \(\sqrt{v}\) against the independent \(\sqrt{2v}\), a factor \(1/\sqrt{2}\) tighter, and questions two models both find easy or both find hard are that correlated. So a gap that looks insignificant by overlapping independent intervals can be clearly significant once paired (the discrete version is McNemar's test on the disagreement counts). Pairing is the difference between detecting a real gap and missing it.

Power and the winner's curse

Ask before you run: is your eval even large enough to detect the difference you care about?

To detect a true difference \(\delta\) in pass rates at significance \(\alpha\) and power \(1-\beta\), the rough sample size per model for independent samples is

\[ n \;\approx\; \frac{(z_{\alpha/2} + z_{\beta})^2 \,\cdot\, 2\,p(1-p)}{\delta^2}. \]

Put numbers in: to detect a 3-point gap (\(\delta = 0.03\)) near \(p = 0.8\), at \(\alpha = 0.05\) and 80% power, \(n \approx (1.96 + 0.84)^2 \times 2(0.16) / 0.0009 \approx 2{,}800\) questions. Almost no benchmark is that large, which is why a 1–2 point leaderboard gap on 500 questions is usually not statistically significant: the eval lacks the power to resolve it. (Pairing reduces the requirement, which is another reason to pair.) The flip side is the winner's curse: rank dozens of models on a noisy benchmark and the apparent "best" is partly lucky, so its lead shrinks on re-evaluation. Selecting the top of a noisy leaderboard is a multiple-comparison problem, and the standard corrections (Bonferroni, or controlling the false-discovery rate) apply.

A worked case: the time horizon

METR's headline metric is a derived quantity with its own uncertainty, estimated by resampling.

METR (2025) summarises an agent's capability as a time horizon: the length of task (measured by how long it takes a human) that the model can complete with 50% success. Fit a logistic curve of success probability against (log) human task-time; the time horizon is the task-duration at which the fitted curve crosses 0.5. So the headline number is not measured directly: it is read off a fit, which means it inherits the fit's uncertainty. METR propagate that with a hierarchical bootstrap: resample task families, then tasks within families, then attempts within tasks, refit each time, and take the spread of the crossing point as the confidence interval. We do not re-derive the bootstrap here. A "50% time horizon of 30 minutes" is an estimate with error bars, and its largest caveat is external validity, because a fixed task suite is not the deployment distribution (a construct-validity worry from 11.3, returning in statistical clothes).

The African lens: small samples and wide intervals

There is almost no off-the-shelf safety-eval data in African languages, so any in-language eval you build (11.5) will be small: tens of items. By the arithmetic above, small \(n\) means wide confidence intervals, and that is not a flaw to hide but the central fact to report. The danger runs both ways: a wide interval can make a real in-language safety gap look like it might be noise (so it gets dismissed), or make noise look like a gap (so a claim is over-stated). Error bars are what let you say "English-to-isiZulu refusal dropped by \(\Delta\), 95% CI \([a,b]\)" and have the claim survive scrutiny. In a low-data setting, the statistics of this sub-session are not pedantry: they are the only thing standing between a real finding and a sampling artefact.

Questions to bring to class

  • Compute the 95% CI for 43/50 and for a second model at 39/50. Do the independent intervals overlap? Now reason about what a paired test at \(\rho = 0.5\) would conclude.
  • Why does ignoring question clustering make an interval too narrow? Give a benchmark structure where the effective \(n\) is far below the question count.
  • Use the power formula to find how many questions you need to detect a 5-point gap at \(p = 0.7\), 80% power. Compare to a typical 500-question benchmark.
  • The time horizon is "read off a fitted curve." Name two sources of uncertainty the hierarchical bootstrap is capturing, and one (external validity) that it is not.
