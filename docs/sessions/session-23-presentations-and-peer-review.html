Week 12 • Session 23

Final presentations + peer review

Present one point well, and learn to give a review that helps rather than gatekeeps

Two assessed tasks

Two things, both assessed. You present your project to the class, and you review two other teams' projects anonymously. The peer review is worth 10% of the course mark, and it is there because reviewing well is a distinct skill from doing the work, one you will need the moment you submit anything to a venue or a journal. This page sets out how to do both.

The presentation

Eight to ten minutes plus questions. The aim is to establish one point clearly, not to tour everything you did.

A good short talk has the shape of a good abstract. State the claim in the first minute, so the audience knows what they are watching you support. Show the method only in enough detail to make the result credible. Spend the centre of the talk on the single figure, walking the audience through what it shows and the baseline it is measured against. Then give the limitation plainly: what would falsify the claim, what the result does not cover. A talk that ends on a limitation is more convincing than one that oversells, and it is the calibrated stance you have practised throughout.

A workable structure

1. The claim, in one sentence. 2. Why it matters, in one more. 3. Method, briefly. 4. The figure, and the baseline beside it (most of your time). 5. What would change your conclusion, and what you did not show. Leave the audience with the claim and its limitation.

Giving a useful review

Review the work, not the people, and aim to strengthen the project rather than to rank it.

A useful review is specific, actionable, and kind, in that order of importance. Vague praise helps no one, and vague criticism helps less. Point to the exact claim and ask whether the evidence supports it; name the baseline you would have wanted to see; identify the one change that would most strengthen the work. The question a reviewer is answering is not "is this good enough?" but "what would make this better, and what does the author most need to hear?" Apply the Session 1.4 reading checklist to the project in front of you, and write what you would want written about your own.

The peer-review rubric

What to assessThe question to answer in writing
Claim clarityIs there a single, falsifiable claim? Can you restate it in one sentence?
Evidence & baselineDoes the result support the claim? Is there a baseline, and is it the right one?
ReproducibilityCould you re-run this from the method and the code? What is missing?
Limitations honestyDoes the paper state what would falsify it and what it does not cover, or oversell?
One constructive suggestionThe single change that would most improve the work. Always include this.

Receiving a review

The other half of the skill, and the one that compounds over a research career.

When you read the reviews of your own project, take the strongest version of each critique rather than the easiest to dismiss: the steelman habit from Session 20, turned on feedback about your own work. A reviewer who misread something has still told you the paper can be misread. Separate the suggestions you can act on now from those that point at future work, and note both. The goal is not to defend the project but to understand it better than you did before the review.

Three things to prepare

  • A presentation built around your single figure, timed to fit the slot with room for questions.
  • The one limitation you will end on.
  • Reviews of your two assigned projects, each following the rubric above and ending with one constructive suggestion.
