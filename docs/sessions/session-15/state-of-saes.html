Week 8 • Session 15.4

The current state of SAEs

The 2024 scaling results, and the 2025 evidence that sparse-autoencoder features do not beat simple probes

What we'll cover

Session 15.3 ended on a tension: sparse autoencoders scale beautifully on reconstruction, but reconstruction was never the goal: the goal is useful features. This sub-session gives that account. In 2024 SAEs looked like a breakthrough; in 2025 careful studies found that SAE-based probes do not reliably beat logistic regression on raw activations once you use a fair baseline, and a major lab publicly deprioritised fundamental SAE research. We work through why the earlier results looked better than they were (a weak-baseline illusion), and land on the non-nihilistic conclusion that SAEs remain a useful exploratory tool, and that the real lesson is methodological: measure against strong baselines. We end with where the frontier moved next: cross-layer transcoders and the attribution graphs built from them.

The downstream test

An SAE is worth the trouble only if its features do something a cheaper method cannot.

OpenAI's own framing (15.3) was that improving the reconstruction-versus-sparsity frontier "is not the ultimate objective." So the test is downstream: take a concrete task (probe an activation for a concept, steer a behaviour, find a capability) and ask whether SAE features beat the obvious baseline of working directly on the raw activations. The natural baseline is a linear probe: a logistic regression trained on the residual stream. If SAE features cannot beat that, the elaborate dictionary has bought interpretability stories but not measurable capability. The 2025 work ran this test, at scale, and the answer was sobering.

The 2025 reassessment

Across 113 probing tasks and four hard regimes, SAE probes did not beat logistic regression on raw activations.

Kantamneni, Engels and colleagues (including Neel Nanda) ran a careful "sparse probing" case study: 113 probing datasets on Gemma-2-9B (replicated on Llama-3.1-8B), across four regimes where interpretable features might be expected to help most: data scarcity, class imbalance, label noise, and covariate shift. Their finding: with a fair "quiver of arrows" protocol, SAE probes do not outperform logistic-regression probes on the raw activations; in standard conditions, adding SAEs slightly lowered the best achievable accuracy. They are careful to call this "not a wholesale critique" (it is one rigorous data point, not a proof SAEs are useless), but it punctures the assumption that SAE features are a free downstream win.

The weak-baseline illusion

The instructive part is why earlier work favoured SAEs. Many positive results compared SAE probes against a weak baseline: for example, max-pooling raw activations across tokens, an operation the activation space does not naturally support (a non-privileged basis, 13.2). Against that strawman, SAEs win. Swap in a fair baseline (e.g. attention-pooled raw activations) and the SAE win-rate collapses, in their numbers from about 20% to under 9%. The methodological "quiver of arrows" lesson is the transferable one: choose the best method on a validation set and report its accuracy on a held-out test set, so you are not crediting SAEs with a win that any competent baseline would also have achieved. Manufacturing a result by handicapping the baseline is the failure the Session 11 statistics warned about, now in interpretability.

A second finding cuts toward the course's African thread: SAE latents often fail out of distribution. Their example is a "living room" latent that fires on the English phrase but is nearly dead on its French translation, a feature that turned out to be bound to the surface language, not the concept. Session 16.4 returns to this; it is direct evidence that interpretability findings can be quietly English-specific.

Google DeepMind's negative results and deprioritisation

When a leading interpretability team publicly steps back from a method, that is data.

Google DeepMind's mechanistic-interpretability team reported negative results for SAEs on downstream tasks: on out-of-distribution harmful-intent probing, dense linear probes were near-perfect while SAE probes were "distinctly worse," and a probe trained on the SAE reconstruction was worse than one on the residual stream, implying SAEs discard task-relevant information. They announced they were deprioritising fundamental SAE research, calling SAEs "unlikely to be a magic bullet" while keeping them as a tool for exploratory work and dataset debugging. This pairing is what this session holds together: Anthropic's triumphant 2024 scaling narrative on one side, the 2025 negative downstream results on the other, both from serious teams.

Three conclusions

None of this says interpretability has failed, or even that SAEs are worthless. It says three things. SAEs are an exploratory tool: they surface candidate features and help debug datasets, which is real value. The reconstruction-sparsity scaling laws are sound but were measuring the wrong target. And the binding norm going forward is strong baselines: any "interpretability method X helps with safety task Y" claim must be tested against the best non-interpretability method, or it is not evidence. Session 16.3 broadens this from "do SAEs work?" to the harder question "does interpretability deliver safety guarantees?" The answer is not yet.

After SAEs: transcoders and attribution graphs

What replaced SAEs at the frontier: transcoder features as the nodes of per-prompt causal graphs.

The DeepMind team deprioritised fundamental SAE research; it did not deprioritise interpretability. Anthropic's 2025 response to the same downstream evidence was to change the dictionary rather than abandon dictionary learning. A cross-layer transcoder is trained to reproduce what the MLP layers compute, rather than to reconstruct activations (the SAE objective), with each feature reading from one layer and writing to all later ones. Trained at up to 30 million features on Claude 3.5 Haiku, a production frontier model, these features become the nodes of an attribution graph: a causal graph, built for one prompt at a time, of which features activated which on the way to an output, with the links checked by the patching interventions of 15.1.

The companion "biology" paper takes the method through case studies on Haiku. Multi-step reasoning: asked for the capital of the state containing Dallas, the model activates Texas features between "Dallas" and "Austin", an intermediate reasoning step you can read off the graph. Planning in poems: the model settles on candidate rhyme words before writing the line, then composes toward them. Multilingual circuits: the same computation runs on a mixture of language-independent concept features and language-specific circuits, the split 13.4's translation evidence pointed at and 16.4 applies to the isiZulu question. The limits travel too: graphs explain one prompt at a time, "error nodes" absorb the computation the transcoder fails to capture, and attention patterns are taken as given rather than explained. 16.3 weighs this frontier-scale result against the 2023 scaling evidence.

Questions to bring to class

  • Why is a logistic-regression probe on raw activations the right baseline for "do SAE features help"? What does it mean if an elaborate SAE cannot beat it?
  • Explain the weak-baseline illusion: how does max-pooling a non-privileged basis manufacture a fake SAE win, and how does a fair protocol remove it?
  • A probe on the SAE reconstruction did worse than a probe on the residual stream. What does that tell you about what the SAE discards?
  • The "living room" latent fires in English but not French. Why is that both a downstream-reliability problem and a clue about English-centric interpretability (16.4)?

Core

Supplementary

• Google DeepMind mechanistic-interpretability team (2025), "Negative Results for SAEs… and Deprioritising SAE Research" (Alignment Forum): the downstream negative results and the deprioritisation decision.

• Wu et al. (2025), "AxBench: Steering LLMs? Even Simple Baselines Outperform Sparse Autoencoders" (arXiv:2501.17148): the benchmark evidence behind this sub-session's caution about SAE steering.

• Ameisen et al. (2025), "Circuit Tracing: Revealing Computational Graphs in Language Models" (transformer-circuits.pub): the cross-layer-transcoder and attribution-graph method.

• Lindsey et al. (2025), "On the Biology of a Large Language Model" (transformer-circuits.pub): the case studies, including the multilingual circuits that 16.4 picks up.
