Week 12 • Session 24

Synthesis & pathways

The four through-lines tied together, what the course argued, and where this work goes next — including the routes that start here

The end of the course

The remaining projects are presented this session, and then the course closes. Twelve weeks ago it opened with a promise: to treat AI safety as a technical subject with real mathematics, to ask "safe for whom?" throughout, and to hold beliefs at the confidence the evidence supports. This sub-session checks that promise against what was actually delivered, and points to where you take it from here.

What we'll cover

A synthesis in four parts: the through-lines that ran through every week, revisited; a calibrated summary of what the course argued and did not claim; the portable skills you should leave with; and concrete pathways into the field, including the ones that begin in Africa.

The four through-lines, revisited

Session 1.1 named four ideas that would recur in almost every session. Here is where each of them ran.

  • The safety–capabilities distinction. What a system can do is not whether we can rely on it, and the organising worry of the field is the widening gap between the two. We tested almost everything against it: scaling laws made capability predictable (Session 2); alignment methods tried to close the gap from the safety side (5–8); robustness, control and evaluation measured how wide it still is (9–12); and the multilingual gap showed the distinction holds one level down, inside a single model, across languages.
  • Idealised → empirical. The course moved deliberately from formal objects you can reason about — objectives, optimisers, proxies, theorems about power-seeking (Weeks 2–4) — to the messy systems we actually have, where those ideas meet noisy practice (Weeks 5–16). Each half informed the other: the theory told you what to look for, and the experiments told you whether it was there.
  • African technical AI safety. Not a single week but a thread: the isiZulu jailbreak as the anchor (1.4), compute and data sovereignty (4, 12), relational ethics (8.3), and the consolidation in Session 18. The honest version held throughout — the centre of gravity is present harms and governance, with two real bridges to frontier safety — rather than a manufactured frontier scene.
  • Intellectual honesty. The hardest and most portable of the four. We steelmanned the skeptics (Session 20), held the field's open problems in plain view, and treated "I don't know, and here is why" as a respectable answer. It is the through-line your project was graded on, and the one that outlasts any particular result.

What the course argued — and what it did not claim

A calibrated summary, in the spirit of the thing it is summarising.

Alignment is not solved, and no technique in this course claimed to solve it. Each manages part of the safety–capabilities gap: RLHF and Constitutional AI shape behaviour but leave the specification problem open; robustness and control bound the damage a misbehaving model can do without guaranteeing it will not try; evaluation and interpretability give evidence about a model rather than proof. Behaviour alone is weak evidence of safety, which is why the course spent weeks on reading internals and building evals, and why even those come fenced with their own open problems — false assurance, construct validity, deception that survives training. The honest position is the one Session 20 reached: take the documented present harms seriously, take the misalignment arguments seriously, and hold each at the confidence its evidence supports. And from where this course is taught, one more claim earns its place: whose languages, whose data and whose access you start from changes what a safety case even has to cover.

What to carry

The skills are more durable than any single result, because the results will change and the skills will not.

  • Read a paper critically. Claim versus evidence, what the eval is, who the population is, what would falsify it — applied to enthusiasts and skeptics alike.
  • Build and read an evaluation. Including the statistics that tell a finding from noise, and the construct-validity question of whether the metric measures the claim.
  • Run an interpretability experiment. And, just as important, distrust it: validate against a baseline, and know what a result does and does not license.
  • Scope honestly. Name the single figure, run the baseline that could kill it, report the error bars, and say what you did not show.
  • Hold a calibrated belief. A credence revised by evidence, not a side chosen by temperament.

Pathways into the field

If you want to go further, there are concrete next steps, and some of the best of them start close to home.

Hands-on technical

ARENA for an engineering deep-dive in interpretability, evals and RL; Neel Nanda's open-problems list and getting-started guides; and the broader map of programmes and fellowships such as MATS.

Conceptual & governance

BlueDot's AI Safety Fundamentals for the alignment and governance curricula, and Hendrycks' Introduction to ML Safety for the technical backbone with problem sets — both drawn on throughout this course.

Starting from Africa

The ILINA Program for African-led frontier-safety research and fellowships; Masakhane and the Deep Learning Indaba for African-language and community work; and UCT's African Hub on AI Safety, Peace and Security for the governance side. Session 18.4 maps the wider ecosystem.

A last word, calibrated

It would be easy to end on either note the field tends to offer: that catastrophe is coming, or that the whole thing is hype. The course has tried to earn a harder position than either. AI safety is at once the most consequential technical problem of the moment and the one most likely to be over-sold, and telling the difference — case by case, claim by claim — is the skill this course exists to build. You now have the tools to do that: to read the evidence, to build the experiment, to steelman the critic, and to say plainly when you do not yet know. Use them on the work that comes next, including your own, and including the parts of the problem that arrive here first.

The course in one sentence

Twenty-four sessions on treating AI safety as a technical subject — from the alignment problem through training, robustness, control, evaluation, governance and interpretability into your own research — asked throughout from a vantage that keeps the question "whose safety, whose risks, whose values?" in view, and held to the standard of believing things only as strongly as the evidence allows.
