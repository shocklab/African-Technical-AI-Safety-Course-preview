Week 9 • Session 18.3

Sovereignty & the decolonial critique

Compute, data and talent sovereignty, the decolonial reading of AI, and what they imply for who can build and audit safe systems

What we'll cover

The multilingual gap is one symptom; this sub-session is about the structure underneath it. We take the three sovereignties (compute, data, talent) that decide who can build frontier systems at all, set out the decolonial reading of AI that names the pattern, note that even the fairness tools meant to fix things do not transfer cleanly, and draw the consequence for safety: the ability to audit and govern these systems is distributed as unequally as the ability to build them.

Three sovereignties

Session 4 argued that compute is the capability floor. From the South, that floor is owned elsewhere, and so are the data and much of the talent.

Compute sovereignty. Training and serving frontier models needs large clusters of accelerators, the energy to run them, and the capital to buy both (Session 4). Almost none of that sits on the African continent. The consequence is not only that African institutions cannot train frontier models; it is that they cannot independently run the experiments needed to study or audit them, and they depend on access granted by others (Session 12 made compute the most tractable governance lever precisely because it is so concentrated). The picture has started to move: Cassava Technologies opened an Nvidia-supplied GPU facility in Johannesburg in June 2025 with 3,000 GPUs, the first stage of a multi-country rollout (reported at around $700 million) that reached roughly 12,000 GPUs by November 2025, sold as GPU-as-a-service. That changes who can rent compute on the continent; it does not yet change who can train a frontier model or run an independent frontier-scale audit, which is the capacity this paragraph is about.

Data sovereignty. The data that trains these systems is drawn from across the world, including from communities that see little of the benefit and have little say in its use. For African languages the asymmetry is double: the data is scarce to begin with (the tokenisation tax of 18.2), and what exists is often collected and held by firms based elsewhere. Who owns the data, who can withdraw it, and who profits from models trained on it are open and contested questions.

Talent sovereignty. Researchers trained in Africa are drawn toward the labs and salaries of the North, so even home-grown capability tends to leave. The field-building organisations of 18.4 exist in large part to counter this, by making it possible to do serious work without emigrating.

Kigali 2025 and the $60bn pledge

At the Global AI Summit on Africa in Kigali (April 2025), African governments endorsed the Africa Declaration on Artificial Intelligence, with a proposed $60 billion Africa AI Fund attached. As of 2026 the fund remains what Session 12 called it: a pledge, with governance undefined and the hardware it would buy still routed through Northern suppliers. The ambition is now continental and political; the three sovereignties above measure the distance between that ambition and the capacity to act on it.

Data colonialism and digital colonialism

A body of work argues these asymmetries are not accidental but structural, and continuous with an older history.

Couldry and Mejias (2019) coined data colonialism to describe how human life is converted into data to be extracted, much as land and labour were extracted under historical colonialism: a new resource frontier, with the periphery again supplying the raw material. Kwet (2019) sharpens this to digital colonialism: US technology firms dominate the digital architecture of the Global South (the software, the hardware, the connectivity), and that ownership of the underlying stack is a form of control that does not require formal empire. These are not metaphors offered loosely; they are claims about who owns the infrastructure on which everything else runs.

The decolonial reading of AI

Decolonial theory enters AI not as an add-on ethics but as a way of seeing the field's power structure clearly.

Mohamed, Png and Isaac (2020), in "Decolonial AI", use decolonial theory as sociotechnical foresight: a lens for anticipating where AI systems will reproduce colonial relations before they do. They name algorithmic coloniality (the ways AI development re-enacts extractive and centralising patterns) and argue for critical technical practice and "reverse pedagogies" that take the periphery's knowledge seriously rather than treating it as a market. Birhane (2020), in "The Algorithmic Colonization of Africa", makes the case concretely for the continent: Western technology monopolies, driven by corporate rather than state agendas, reproduce colonial dynamics through the AI systems they export and the data they gather. Her relational-ethics work (2021), which you met in Session 8.3, is the constructive other half: if harm is relational and contextual, then ethics cannot be a checklist applied from outside.

The West-centric fairness toolkit

Sambasivan and colleagues (2021), in "Re-imagining Algorithmic Fairness in India and Beyond", show that the standard algorithmic-fairness toolkit is built on assumptions that fail outside the West: the protected attributes it watches, the data infrastructures it presumes, and the legal context it borrows from are all specific to the United States. Simply porting a fairness method to India, they argue, is "window dressing" unless the data and models are re-contextualised and the affected communities (along axes such as caste, gender and religion) are given real power. The lesson generalises to Africa: the responsible-AI tools meant to address present harms themselves need rebuilding for the South, which is one more thing the local ecosystem of 18.4 is for.

Sovereignty and the capacity to audit

Tie this back to the course's own machinery: auditing a model is a function of access, and access is exactly what sovereignty governs.

Session 16.4 made an argument from the interpretability auditing game: in that study the team restricted to API access alone failed to find a model's hidden objective, while teams with weight access succeeded. Access tier decided whether an audit was even possible. Put that next to the three sovereignties and the consequence is direct. If compute, model weights, and the data are concentrated in a handful of Northern firms, then the capacity to audit, evaluate, and govern frontier systems is concentrated in the same place. The Global South is structurally placed on the low-access tier (able to use these systems, far less able to inspect them), and that is a safety problem, not only an equity one, because the people most exposed to a system's failures are the least able to check it in advance.

The scope of the critique

The decolonial critique is, in the main, an account of present and structural harms and of power: who owns what, who benefits, who is heard. That is its strength and its proper scope. It is not a frontier-alignment research programme, and reading it as one would distort it. The connection it makes to frontier safety is the access argument above: sovereignty determines who can audit, and auditing is a frontier-safety capability. Keep the critique and the alignment agenda distinct, and let the access argument connect them.

Questions to bring to class

  • Define compute, data and talent sovereignty. Which does Session 12 argue is the most tractable governance lever, and why?
  • "Data colonialism" and "digital colonialism" are distinct claims. State each, and say what would count as evidence for or against it.
  • Use the 16.4 auditing-game result to argue that sovereignty is a safety issue, not only a fairness one. Where is that argument strongest, and where does it overreach?
  • Sambasivan et al. call ported fairness methods "window dressing". What would re-contextualising a fairness method for an African setting actually require?

Readings

Core

• Mohamed, S., Png, M.-T. & Isaac, W. (2020), "Decolonial AI: Decolonial Theory as Sociotechnical Foresight in Artificial Intelligence" (arXiv:2007.04068; Philosophy & Technology 33(4)). The framing piece.

• Birhane, A. (2020), "The Algorithmic Colonization of Africa" (SCRIPTed 17(2), open access). The concrete African case.

Supplementary

• Sambasivan, N. et al. (2021), "Re-imagining Algorithmic Fairness in India and Beyond" (arXiv:2101.09995; FAccT '21). Why the fairness toolkit does not transfer.

• Kwet, M. (2019), "Digital colonialism: US empire and the new imperialism in the Global South" (Race & Class 60(4)). Ownership of the digital stack. Couldry & Mejias, The Costs of Connection (Stanford UP, 2019), is the book-length "data colonialism" argument.
