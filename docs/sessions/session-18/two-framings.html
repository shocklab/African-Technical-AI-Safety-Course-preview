Week 9 • Session 18.1

Two framings of AI risk

Present harms or existential risk? Whose safety, whose risks, whose values?

Session 18 — AI safety from the Global South

This is the session the course's African through-line has been building toward. It consolidates four threads you have already met: the isiZulu jailbreak (1.4, 9), compute and data sovereignty (4, 12), relational ethics (8.3), and the access-tier argument for auditing (16.4). Together they pose one question: what does AI safety look like when you ask it from the Global South? The answer is neither a separate field nor a diversity add-on, and this first sub-session sets the frame for the three that follow.

What we'll cover

Two framings divide the field: AI risk as documented present harm, and AI risk as speculative existential catastrophe. The split is real, it is political, and it shapes whose work gets called "AI safety" at all. This sub-session lays out both framings, shows why the Global South sharpens rather than settles the argument, and states plainly where this session stands.

Two framings of AI risk

The same three words, "AI safety", name two quite different research programmes, and most public argument is really a fight over which one they should name.

The present-harms framing locates the danger in systems deployed now: biased decisions, exploited data labourers, mass surveillance, the environmental cost of training and inference, the concentration of power in a few firms, and the erosion of the information ecosystem. The harms are documented, measurable, and unevenly distributed. This is the register of Timnit Gebru and the DAIR institute, of Abeba Birhane, and of much of the fairness and accountability literature.

The existential-risk framing locates the danger in future systems: misaligned objectives, loss of control, deception that survives training. The harms are mostly anticipated rather than observed, and the argument runs through the alignment problem you spent Weeks 2 to 8 on. This is the register of most frontier-lab safety teams and the bulk of this course's taught core.

Session 1.2's three-way cut maps onto both: misuse and systemic risk are largely the present-harms register, misalignment is largely the existential one. That taxonomy held all three at once, and this session does the same.

The dispute between the camps

The two camps do more than emphasise different risks; each accuses the other of getting safety wrong.

The present-harms camp argues that the existential framing diverts attention and money from harms happening now, and that its language of "safety" and "benefiting humanity" can serve the commercial interests of the labs that deploy it. You will meet the sharpest versions of this argument in Session 20: the critique that the AGI-risk narrative advances the incumbents who claim to be racing toward it. The existential camp replies that present harms, however real, do not exhaust the risk, and that a problem need not be observed yet to be worth preparing for.

The dispute is not resolved here, and it is not an empty one. Both registers point at real dangers; treating either as the whole of "AI safety" erases the other. What the Global South adds is a vantage point rather than a third camp, one from which the choice between the two looks different.

The Global South as a vantage point

"Whose safety, whose risks, whose values?" is not a slogan here; it changes which risks are visible and which levers are available.

Seen from most of the African continent, the present harms are concentrated and the means of redress are thin: the data that trains these systems is extracted with little benefit returned, the compute that runs them sits elsewhere (Session 4), and the governance levers that might constrain them are weakest where the exposure is highest (Session 12). On the present-harms reading, the South is the place the harms fall hardest.

But the South is not only on the present-harms side of the ledger, and this is the part that is easy to miss. In 2023, the low-resource-language jailbreak of 1.4 showed a frontier model's safety collapsing, and it was found by asking the model a question in the languages of the South. That was an existential-framing failure (a safety property that did not generalise) surfaced by a Global-South vantage the English-speaking centre did not occupy. The South, in other words, sits on both sides of the framing it is usually assigned to only one of.

The stance this session defends

Honesty first, because the course grades you on it: most African and Global-South AI work is present-harms work, not a frontier-safety scene inflated beyond its real size.

The centre of gravity of AI work across the Global South is present harms, fairness, access, and governance. The institutions you will meet in 18.4 (the language collectives, the dataset funds, the governance think tanks) operate overwhelmingly in that register. Claiming a thriving African frontier-alignment community would be a manufactured angle.

Two bridges carry the frontier-safety weight. The first is institutional (18.4): the ILINA Program, an African-led organisation whose research focus is frontier and catastrophic-risk safety, not an aspiration, with a small field-building layer growing beside it in groups such as AI Safety South Africa. The second is the multilingual safety gap (18.2): a frontier-safety vulnerability that a Southern perspective made visible. The Southern contribution to frontier safety is best understood as re-defining what counts as a safety problem and whose reality the safety case must cover, not as a parallel set of frontier labs.

The overloaded word "safety"

The word "safety" is overloaded, and African institutions use it in a particular way. When the African Union's Continental AI Strategy or UCT's African Hub on AI Safety, Peace and Security say "AI safety", they mean governance, societal harms, and peace-and-security, not frontier alignment in the Week-2-to-8 sense. The ILINA Program and the field-builders around it use it in the frontier sense. The language collectives build the capability substrate that makes any of it possible. Reading all three as if they meant the same thing would mislead you, so the session keeps the three senses apart and tells you which is which.

Questions to bring to class

  • State the present-harms and existential-risk framings in one sentence each. Which does the isiZulu jailbreak belong to, and why is that the interesting answer?
  • Is "the existential framing distracts from present harms" an argument about evidence, about resources, or about politics? Does the answer change how you would respond to it?
  • The session claims the Global South sits on both sides of the framing. Give one present-harms example and one frontier-safety example from the African context.
  • Why might calling an African governance institute an "AI safety" body mislead a reader who knows only the frontier sense of the term?
