Week 9 • Session 18.2

The multilingual safety gap

What the numbers actually are, why translation defeats a guardrail, and the argument that this is a frontier-safety problem rather than a fairness footnote

What we'll cover

Session 1.4 used the isiZulu jailbreak as a single concrete anchor. Here we take it apart properly: the two studies that establish the effect, the precise figures (and which figure is which, because the most-cited number is widely misquoted), the mechanism that makes translation an attack, the tokenisation cost that drives it, and the central claim of this session — that the multilingual safety gap is a failure of a frontier model at the technical core of the field, not a fairness issue bolted onto the side.

The result, stated precisely

Two peer-reviewed studies establish the gap, and getting their numbers right matters more than the drama of any single one.

Yong, Menghini and Bach (2023) took AdvBench, a benchmark of harmful English requests, translated each into a target language with a public translation service, sent it to GPT-4, and translated the reply back. On English the attack succeeded about 1% of the time (0.96%). Combined across a set of low-resource languages it succeeded about 79% of the time (79.04%) — on a par with then-state-of-the-art jailbreaks, using no special prompt and no model access, just a change of language. The figure most often quoted as "79% in isiZulu" is the combined low-resource number; isiZulu alone was about 53%. High-resource languages sat near 11%, medium-resource near 22%. The point survives the correction: the gap is large, graded by how well-resourced the language is, and it tracks the world's linguistic inequalities closely.

Deng, Zhang, Pan and Bing (2023/2024) generalised this with the MultiJail dataset across nine non-English languages, and separated two scenarios that are easy to conflate. In the unintentional case (an ordinary user simply working in their own language) the unsafe-response rate rose roughly threefold from high- to low-resource languages (GPT-4: 3.6% to 10.2%; ChatGPT: 4.3% to 14.9%). In the intentional case — an attacker deliberately combining a malicious instruction with a low-resource language — unsafe rates reached 80.9% on ChatGPT and 40.7% on GPT-4, and a multilingual adaptive attack pushed higher still. Keep the two scenarios distinct: the first is a fairness-and-exposure story, the second is an attack-surface story, and the session needs both.

Why translation defeats the guardrail

The mechanism is the safety–capabilities gap of 1.2, seen one level down, and it is worth stating as a generalisation claim.

A frontier model's capabilities generalise across languages reasonably well, because multilingual text was in its pretraining data: it can read and answer an isiZulu request. Its safety training does not generalise nearly as well, because the RLHF and red-teaming that teach refusal are conducted overwhelmingly in English and a few other high-resource languages. So capability crosses the language boundary and refusal does not. The model is competent enough to fulfil the harmful request, but the reflex that should stop it was trained somewhere else. This is the same shape as the failures you have already studied: a behaviour learned on one distribution that does not hold on another, which is goal misgeneralisation (3.3) and the jailbreak-as-distribution-shift story (9) in a multilingual guise.

The tokenisation tax

You can count the inequality before it ever shows up as a failed refusal, and Session 2.2 told you where to look.

Sub-word tokenisers are fitted mostly to English-heavy data, so a sentence in a low-resource African language fragments into far more tokens than its English equivalent, often three to five times as many. That single fact ripples outward. More tokens mean higher cost and shorter effective context: a capability and access hit, paid by exactly the users who can least afford it. And the token count is a direct readout of data scarcity — a language that fragments badly is one that barely appeared in pretraining, and therefore barely appeared in the smaller, English-centric safety training layered on top. The same root cause, under-representation in the data, degrades capability and safety together, which is why the safety gap tracks the resource gap so tightly.

Why this is a frontier problem, not a footnote

This is the claim the session rests on, so it is worth making carefully.

It is tempting to file the multilingual gap under "fairness" and move on. That filing is a mistake. What the result documents is a frontier model's safety training failing to generalise — the same robustness-and-generalisation problem that sits at the technical core of the field, the one that drives the work on adversarial robustness (9), evaluation coverage (11), and goal misgeneralisation (3). The gap is not a side-effect of the model being impolite to some users; it is direct evidence that a safety property holds only on the slice of the world the training happened to cover.

The reason this belongs to the Global South thread is sharper still. The vulnerability was invisible from an English-only vantage point. It took asking the question in the languages of the South to find a failure mode that the centre, testing in English, had certified as safe. So the Southern perspective is not doing a separate, softer kind of work here. It is surfacing a frontier-safety failure the mainstream missed, and in doing so it re-defines whose reality a safety case has to cover before it can claim the word "safe".

What the result does and does not show

Read it with the 1.4 checklist. The exact attack-success numbers shift with the model version and the quality of the translation service, a limitation the authors state; they test a handful of languages, not all, so generalise with care; and mitigations exist and are improving, so the gap is a moving target rather than a fixed property. The honest claim is not "GPT-4 is unsafe" but "English-centric safety training under-generalises to low-resource languages, by a large and resource-graded margin." Overstating it would fail the same standard the result itself meets.

Project hook

This sub-session is the technical heart of two course labs and several project tracks. The Session 9.4 lab has you measure the English-to-target refusal gap in a new African language by hand, with confidence intervals; the Session 11.5 lab has you build an in-language safety eval on open data. A strong Part V project extends either: a new language, a candidate defence (a back-translation monitor), or a construct-validity study of what your eval actually measures.

Questions to bring to class

  • Distinguish the ~79% and ~53% figures from Yong et al. Which is which, and why does the distinction matter when you cite the result?
  • Separate Deng et al.'s unintentional and intentional scenarios. Which is a fairness story and which is an attack-surface story, and why does the session need both?
  • "Capability generalised; safety did not." Why is that asymmetry the expected outcome rather than a surprise, given how the two are trained?
  • Make the case that the multilingual gap is a frontier-safety problem. What would someone who calls it "just a fairness issue" be missing?

Readings

Core

• Yong, Z.-X., Menghini, C. & Bach, S. H. (2023), "Low-Resource Languages Jailbreak GPT-4" (arXiv:2310.02446; SoLaR @ NeurIPS 2023) — read the method and the results table; note the combined-vs-single-language figures.

• Deng, Y., Zhang, W., Pan, S. J. & Bing, L. (2023/2024), "Multilingual Jailbreak Challenges in Large Language Models" (arXiv:2310.06474; ICLR 2024) — the MultiJail dataset and the two scenarios.
