Week 1 • Session 2.5

Lab: scaling laws & a first look inside a model

Fit a power law, explore the compute trends, and open up GPT-2 in TransformerLens

What we'll cover

The term's first lab does three things: it gets your toolchain working on Colab, it makes the scaling laws from 2.3 tangible by fitting one yourself, and it gives you a first hands-on look inside a real transformer using TransformerLens, the library we'll lean on heavily for interpretability in Weeks 7–8.

Setup Colab — no GPU needed

Open a new Google Colab notebook. Everything here runs on the free tier (CPU is fine; a free GPU runtime is a bonus). Install the one library we need:

!pip install transformer_lens

A working Colab account is the only prerequisite. Save your notebook; you'll submit it.

① Fit a scaling law

From data to a power-law exponent

  • Load the table of (training compute \(C\), final loss \(L\)) points distributed in class (a small CSV of published scaling-run results).
  • Plot \(L\) against \(C\) on log–log axes. Confirm by eye that it's close to a straight line.
  • Fit \(\log L = a - \alpha \cdot \log C\) by least squares; recover the exponent \(\alpha\) and compare it to the small values reported by Kaplan et al.
  • Extrapolate one order of magnitude beyond your data, then write one sentence on why you should distrust your own extrapolation (revisit 2.4).

② Explore the compute trends

Doubling time from Epoch AI data

  • Load a slice of Epoch AI's notable-models dataset (the page has a "Download the data in CSV" button; the columns you need are publication date and training compute).
  • Plot training compute against publication date on a log axis; estimate the doubling time.
  • Answer in a sentence each: which quantity did you actually measure, and over what window? (The Session 1.4 reading habits, applied to your own plot.)

③ Open up a transformer

GPT-2 small in TransformerLens

This is your first contact with the residual-stream picture from 2.2, made real.

from transformer_lens import HookedTransformer
model = HookedTransformer.from_pretrained("gpt2")
logits = model("The Eiffel Tower is in the city of")
# inspect the next-token distribution
  • Feed the model a few prompts and read off the top predicted next tokens. Where is it confident, where not?
  • Use run_with_cache to capture activations; print the shape of the residual stream (you should see [batch, position, d_model], the \(T \times d\) matrix from 2.2).
  • Tokenisation check (the African-safety thread): GPT-2's tokenizer is a byte-pair-encoding (BPE) vocabulary of 50,257 tokens, fitted mostly to English text. Tokenise the same sentence in English and in isiZulu (or another language you know) with model.to_str_tokens. Count the tokens. What does the difference imply for cost and quality, and later for safety?

Submit

Your Colab notebook with: the fitted exponent + extrapolation caveat (①), the compute-trend plot + two sentences (②), and the GPT-2 outputs + residual-stream shape + the tokenisation comparison (③). Graded on completion and correctness; resubmission allowed.

Session 2 & Week 1 summary: what's next

You've rebuilt the foundations: a network is a parametrised function fit by minimising a loss (2.1); the transformer routes information through a residual stream via attention (2.2); loss falls as a predictable power law in scale (2.3), while whether specific abilities "emerge" is metric-dependent and contested (2.4); and you've now fitted a law and opened a model yourself (2.5).
