🧮 From Neurons to Networks

A fast, maths-honest recap: what a network computes, and what training actually does

🧮 Week 2 — deep learning & scaling laws

To reason about how these systems fail, you need a working picture of how they're built. This session rebuilds that picture at the level a mathematician wants — functions, parameters, gradients — then shows why capability scales so predictably with size.

This session's sub-sessions: 2.1 from neurons to networks · 2.2 inside the transformer · 2.3 neural scaling laws · 2.4 pace of progress & the emergence debate · 2.5 lab (Colab).

🎯 What we'll cover

You have met neural networks before, but cohorts vary, so we set a common, precise baseline. This sub-session frames a network as a parametrised function fit by gradient descent — nothing mystical — and pins down the few ideas (the loss, the gradient, the objective) that the safety arguments later hang on.

📐 A network is a parametrised function

Strip away the biology metaphor and a neural network is just a function \( f_\theta : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m \) with a great many parameters \( \theta \).

From a neuron to a layer

Start with a single neuron: it takes an input vector \( x \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{in}}} \), forms a weighted sum with its own weight vector \( w \) and bias \( b \), and passes the result through a nonlinearity — producing one scalar, \( a = \sigma(w \cdot x + b) \). Now stack \( d_{\text{out}} \) neurons side by side and collect their weight vectors as the rows of a matrix \( W \). The whole layer then computes a vector in one shot:

\[ h = \sigma(Wx + b), \qquad W \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{out}} \times d_{\text{in}}}, \quad b,\, h \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{out}}} \]

Here \( \sigma \) is applied elementwise — the same scalar function to each component — and the standard choice is ReLU, \( \sigma(z) = \max(0, z) \). A deep network chains \( L \) such layers, each feeding the next:

\[ h^{(\ell)} = \sigma\!\left(W^{(\ell)} h^{(\ell-1)} + b^{(\ell)}\right), \qquad h^{(0)} = x \]

and the full collection of weights and biases \( \{W^{(\ell)},\, b^{(\ell)}\} \) is exactly what we bundled into the parameters \( \theta \). Without \( \sigma \) the composition of linear maps would collapse to a single linear map — the nonlinearity is what lets depth buy expressive power. By the universal-approximation results such a network can approximate essentially any function; the real engineering question is whether gradient descent can find parameters that do so.

🎯 Training = minimising a loss

"Learning" is optimisation. We choose a loss that scores predictions against targets and descend it.

The loop, precisely

  • Loss. A scalar \( L(\theta) \) measuring how wrong the model is on data — for language models, the cross-entropy of the predicted next-token distribution against the true token.
  • Gradient. Compute \( \nabla_\theta L \) by back-propagation (the chain rule, applied layer by layer).
  • Step. Update \( \theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta\, \nabla_\theta L \) for a small learning rate \( \eta \) (in practice, a variant like Adam). Each step estimates the gradient on a mini-batch — a small random subset of the data, far cheaper than using all of it — and we repeat for a very long time.

That is the whole of it. Everything a frontier model "knows" is the low-loss configuration of \( \theta \) this process settles into on its training data.

💡 What the language-model loss actually is

A language model is trained to predict the next token — a chunk of text, defined precisely in Session 2.2 — given the tokens before it. At each position it outputs a probability distribution \( p_\theta \) over the whole vocabulary (the softmax of its output scores; softmax is defined in 2.2), and the loss is the cross-entropy:

\[ L = -\frac{1}{T} \sum_i \log p_\theta\!\left(t_i \mid t_{<i}\right) \]

where \( t_i \) is the true token at position \( i \) and \( t_{<i} \) all tokens before it. (General cross-entropy is \( -\sum p_{\text{true}} \log p_\theta \); because the true "distribution" puts all its mass on the one token that actually occurred, the sum collapses to the negative log-probability of that token.) So minimising \( L \) just means making the correct token as probable as possible. Two things to carry forward: this is exactly the "loss" that falls as a power law in Session 2.3; and the model is never told how to be right, only scored on the probability it placed on what actually came next.

⚠️ Why this framing matters for safety

🎯 We get to choose the loss — not the behaviour

This is the hinge the rest of the course turns on. We do not program behaviour; we specify a loss and an optimiser, and behaviour is whatever minimises that loss on that data. The model optimises the objective we wrote down, which is only ever a proxy for what we meant. Every alignment failure you'll study is, at root, a gap between those two — surfaced by an optimiser doing exactly its job.

💡 Two consequences to keep

First, the objective is a specification problem: get the loss subtly wrong and you get subtly wrong behaviour, at scale (Session 3, Goodhart). Second, the parameters are opaque: gradient descent finds a solution, not an interpretable one — which is exactly why we need mechanistic interpretability (Weeks 7–8) to read back out what the network actually learned.
