The control paradigm

Trusted versus untrusted models, and the control evaluation as a red-team game

What we'll cover

Everything so far has tried to make a model want the right thing: RLHF, Constitutional AI, oversight. AI control (Greenblatt, Shlegeris et al., 2023) takes the opposite stance: assume that effort might have failed, that the capable model you are deploying may be a competent adversary, and ask whether you can build a protocol that stays safe anyway. This sub-session defines the paradigm (trusted versus untrusted models, the control evaluation as a red-team/blue-team game) and derives the first quantitative result by hand, showing why a naïve "audit some of it" strategy is scale-invariantly weak. This gives a precise reading of the word "safety": a probability against a specified attack, not a property of the model.

A different bet from alignment

Oversight tries to make the model trustworthy. Control assumes you failed, and contains the damage.

Session 8.1 surveyed scalable oversight (debate, amplification, weak-to-strong), all aimed at aligning a model you cannot directly check. Control starts from the failure case those methods are trying to avoid. It consumes the threat model of Session 3 (a model that is scheming, deceptively aligned, pursuing a goal of its own and willing to act against you) and asks a narrower, more pessimistic question: given that the model might be like that, can a deployment protocol guarantee that the damage stays bounded? You are not trying to trust the model. You are trying to make trust unnecessary. That reframing pairs with oversight rather than replacing it: oversight aims to raise the chance the model is safe; control aims to limit the cost if it is not.

Trusted and untrusted models

The protocol's resources are three. An untrusted model \(U\) (in the paper, GPT-4): capable enough to do the useful work, and capable enough that you cannot rule out that it is scheming. A trusted model \(T\) (GPT-3.5): weaker, but understood well enough (or too weak to plot effectively) that you are confident it is not subverting you. And scarce trusted high-quality labour \(H\): expensive human review you can afford on only a small fraction of cases. The art of a control protocol is to spend a lot of cheap, capable-but-untrusted \(U\), a little weak-but-trusted \(T\), and a tiny amount of \(H\), and still come out safe. "Trusted" is a bet about which models are too weak or too well-understood to be a threat, not a moral judgement.

The testbed: backdoors in code

A concrete game makes the abstract paradigm measurable.

Greenblatt et al. instantiate control on a programming task. \(U\) is asked to solve competitive-programming problems (the APPS dataset); the blue team wants to deploy \(U\)'s solutions because they are good, but must never ship a backdoor: code that passes all the visible tests yet contains an input on which it deliberately fails, which \(U\) (playing the adversary) could later exploit. The red team controls \(U\) and tries to slip in a backdoor that survives review. The blue team designs the protocol: when to trust \(U\)'s code, when to have \(T\) check it, when to spend a unit of \(H\) auditing. The game runs over a long sequence of problems, and there are two numbers to trade off:

Safety and usefulness, defined

  • Safety = the probability the blue team gets through the whole sequence (5,000 problems) without ever submitting a backdoor, measured against the red team's best attack policy. It is a worst-case probability, not an average-case score.
  • Usefulness = how much of \(U\)'s capability you capture, rescaled so that using only \(T\) scores \(0\) and using \(U\) with no safety measures scores \(1\).

Two trivial baselines bound the problem. Never use \(U\): perfectly safe, but usefulness \(0\), forfeiting the capable model entirely. Use \(U\) freely, audit a small fraction up front: usefulness \(\approx 1\), but safety only around 15% in the paper's evaluation, against a red team that backdoors rarely; the derivation below shows why in a simplified model. The whole research question lives between these two.

Deriving the safety of upfront auditing

The simplest protocol's safety can be computed in closed form, and it does not improve with scale.

Take the upfront-auditing baseline: the blue team spends its entire audit budget on the first fraction \(q\) of problems, checking each with \(H\) (so any backdoor there is caught and the model is busted), and after the budget runs out it cannot audit at all. The red team inserts a backdoor on each problem independently with probability \(p\). Walk through the outcomes over \(N\) problems:

  • If a backdoor lands in the audited prefix (the first \(qN\) problems), the blue team catches it; blue wins.
  • If no backdoor lands in the prefix but at least one lands later, an un-audited backdoor is submitted; red wins.

So the red team's win probability is

\[ P_{\text{red}} = \underbrace{(1-p)^{qN}}_{\text{prefix clean}} \;\Big(\,1 - \underbrace{(1-p)^{(1-q)N}}_{\text{suffix also clean}}\,\Big). \]

Now the key observation. Let \(\lambda = pN\) be the expected number of backdoors the red team plants over the whole run. As \(N\) grows with \(\lambda\) fixed, \((1-p)^{qN} \to e^{-q\lambda}\) and \((1-p)^{(1-q)N} \to e^{-(1-q)\lambda}\), so

\[ P_{\text{red}} \;\longrightarrow\; e^{-q\lambda}\,\big(1 - e^{-(1-q)\lambda}\big), \]

which depends only on the audit fraction \(q\) and the attack intensity \(\lambda\), not on \(N\). The red team optimises over \(\lambda\) (equivalently, picks how rarely to backdoor), and because the expression is scale-free, making the deployment bigger does not help the defender: double the number of problems and the attacker halves \(p\) to keep \(\lambda\) where it wants it. For a small audit budget like \(q = 0.02\), the optimum is \(\lambda \approx 4\), where \(P_{\text{red}} \approx 0.90\): the blue team is only about 10% safe. The paper's fuller model of the same game reports 15%; the toy expression gives the mechanism and the order of magnitude, not the exact number. That is the precise sense in which upfront auditing fails, and it motivates everything in 10.3, where the budget is spent adaptively, on the cases that look suspicious, instead of on a fixed prefix.

Safety numbers as game values

This number is not a property of GPT-4. It is the value of a game: the blue protocol against the red team's best response, under stated rules. Change the protocol and it moves; change the attacker's options and it moves again. Every safety number in this session is of this form: a min–max probability against a specified adversary class, not a measurement of how dangerous the model is. Keeping that distinction in view is a central habit the control literature teaches, and 10.4 makes it an explicit exercise.

Control as a red-team/blue-team evaluation

You estimate a protocol's safety by playing the adversary.

The reason control yields numbers at all is that it is evaluated as a game. The blue team fixes a protocol; a human red team (standing in for a scheming \(U\)) is given the protocol and tries its hardest to get a backdoor through; safety is estimated from how often it succeeds. This is a conservative methodology: you assume the model is as adversarial as your red team can simulate, including coordinating with copies of itself. A control safety number is only as good as the attacks the red team thought of, a limitation the authors state plainly and we return to in 10.4.

The African-lens angle: who holds the keys?

A control protocol presupposes an actor who owns the stack: who chooses the trusted model \(T\), commands the scarce trusted labour \(H\), and sits between the untrusted model and the world. For a frontier model served from abroad as an API, the downstream operator in Lagos or Cape Town owns none of these: they receive \(U\)'s outputs and cannot interpose a control protocol at all. Control is an affordance of whoever runs the deployment, and that is rarely the country where the model is used. The only lever a recipient of an open-weight model has is to harden it directly. As 10.4 shows, that is the most fragile and compute-hungry safeguard of all. So the people with least control over the stack are pushed toward the technique that is hardest to keep working.

Questions to bring to class

  • State the difference between control and oversight (Session 8.1) in one sentence each. Why are they complements rather than competitors?
  • Derive \(P_{\text{red}}\) for upfront auditing yourself, then take the \(N \to \infty\) limit with \(\lambda = pN\) fixed. Where exactly does \(N\) drop out, and what does that mean for a defender who hopes scale will help?
  • Why is "trusted" a claim about a model's capability rather than its values? What would make you reclassify a trusted model as untrusted?
  • A control safety number is a min–max against a red team. Give a concrete attack a red team might miss, and say what that omission does to the reported safety.

Readings

Core

• Greenblatt, Shlegeris, Sachan & Roger (2023), "AI Control: Improving Safety Despite Intentional Subversion" (arXiv:2312.06942). §1–2 for the paradigm, the testbed, and the baselines.

Supplementary

• Hendrycks, "Introduction to AI Safety, Ethics, and Society", §3.2 (Monitoring) (aisafetybook.com). Monitoring as a safety mechanism, for context.

• Korbak et al. (2025), "A sketch of an AI control safety case" (arXiv:2501.17315): what a deployment argument built on control, rather than alignment, actually looks like.
