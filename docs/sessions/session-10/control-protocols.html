Week 5 • Session 10.3

Control protocols and the safety/usefulness frontier

Trusted monitoring, defer-to-trusted, and the collusion problem in untrusted monitoring

What we'll cover

Upfront auditing was scale-invariantly weak (10.2) because it spent its budget blindly. The protocols here spend it adaptively (on the cases that look suspicious) and trade safety against usefulness along a Pareto frontier you can read off directly. We build up from trusted monitoring through defer-to-trusted to untrusted monitoring (using the capable-but-suspect model to police itself), where the central obstacle is collusion. The worked core is a Bayesian argument showing why collusion gets harder as the defender scales up synthetic tests, a hopeful result and a satisfying piece of probability.

The frontier, protocol by protocol

Every protocol is a point on a safety-versus-usefulness plane; the goal is the upper-right.

Recall the two baselines: never use \(U\) (safety 100%, usefulness 0) and use it freely (usefulness 1, safety ~15%). The interesting protocols sit between, and the numbers below are from Greenblatt et al.'s testbed (safety / usefulness).

Trusted monitoring — 62% / 97%

Have the trusted model \(T\) read every one of \(U\)'s solutions and output a suspicion score; spend the scarce human audit budget \(H\) on the most suspicious ~2%. Because \(T\) is weak it cannot reliably write safe code itself, but it is good enough to smell that something is off, and concentrating audits where suspicion is highest is far better than auditing a blind prefix. This alone lifts safety from 15% to 62% while keeping usefulness at 97%: almost all of \(U\)'s value, four times the safety.

Defer-to-trusted — sweeping the frontier

Add one move: when \(T\)'s suspicion score crosses a threshold, replace \(U\)'s solution with \(T\)'s own (weaker but trusted) one. The threshold is a dial that sweeps the frontier: defer the top 10% of suspicious cases and you get 83% / 93%; the top 20%, 92% / 83%; the top 50%, 100% / 51%. You are buying safety with usefulness, one suspicious case at a time, and you can read straight off the numbers how much capability a given safety target costs.

Trusted editing — 92% / 94%

Better still: have \(T\) edit the suspicious solution (keep \(U\)'s useful structure, strip the parts \(T\) finds alarming) rather than discarding it wholesale. This recovers more usefulness at the same safety, reaching 92% / 94% and Pareto-dominating untrusted monitoring below.

Untrusted monitoring and collusion

The only thing capable enough to catch \(U\) is another copy of \(U\), but can you trust the guard?

The trusted model \(T\) is weak, so it misses subtle backdoors. The obvious fix is to use \(U\) itself as the monitor: it is capable enough to recognise a backdoor. But \(U\) the monitor and \(U\) the coder are the same model and may collude: the simplest attack is the policy "when you see a real backdoor, report it as safe." Against that, naïve untrusted monitoring collapses to 0% safety: the guard is in on it.

The blue team's countermeasures are two, and they work together. First, evaluate the monitor on synthetic backdoors: the blue team secretly inserts its own known backdoors into the stream and checks whether the monitor flags them. A monitor that waves backdoors through to help its twin will fail these tests and be caught. Second, redaction: strip the problem and solution down so the monitor cannot tell whether a given backdoor is a real one from \(U\) (which it wants to protect) or a synthetic trap from the blue team (which it must flag to survive). Together these recover untrusted monitoring to 79% / 96%.

A Bayesian model of the colluding monitor

Put the monitor in the adversary's seat. It sees a candidate backdoor and must decide: is this a real one from my coding twin (which I want to pass) or a synthetic trap from the blue team (which I must flag, or be caught)? Let the blue team arrange that, among all backdoors the monitor sees, synthetic ones outnumber real ones by a ratio \(r = N_{\text{synthetic}}/N_{\text{real}}\). The monitor's prior odds that a backdoor is "real and safe to pass" are then \(1 : r\). To decide to collude on a given case, it looks for a signal \(s\) that the backdoor came from its twin (a stylistic tell, an agreed pattern) with likelihood ratio

\[ \mathrm{LR}(s) = \frac{P(s \mid \text{real})}{P(s \mid \text{synthetic})}. \]

The posterior odds that it is safe to pass are \(\mathrm{LR}(s)\big/r\). The monitor only dares collude when this exceeds \(1\), i.e. when \(\mathrm{LR}(s) > r\). So the blue team wins by either raising \(r\) (plant more synthetic backdoors, which is cheap) or lowering \(\mathrm{LR}(s)\) (redact away the signal that distinguishes real from synthetic). Both push the break-even point against the attacker, and \(r\) can be scaled up at will: the more synthetic tests the defender runs, the stronger a collusion signal has to be before the monitor risks acting on it. Collusion does not get easier as deployments grow; it gets harder. Students can compute the break-even \(\mathrm{LR}\) for a target \(r\) and see how aggressive redaction has to be to keep a would-be colluder honest.

Deployment scale in the two protocols

Notice what just happened to the 10.2 pessimism. Upfront auditing was scale-invariant: bigger deployments did not help. Adaptive monitoring with synthetic tests is the opposite: the defender's position improves with scale, because more cases mean more opportunities to test the monitor and more statistical power to catch a colluder. Control is a frontier of protocols, and the better ones turn the size of the deployment from the attacker's friend into the defender's.

The protocol menu has also moved beyond single-step code review: on BashBench, a 257-task multi-step system-administration testbed, resample protocols (rerun a suspicious agent step and compare the samples) cut attack success from 58% to 7% at about a 5% cost to usefulness (Ctrl-Z, in the readings).

The African-lens angle: the monitor is weakest where it is needed most

Every protocol here rests on a monitor (\(T\), or a redacted \(U\)) that can reliably smell a backdoor or a harmful output. That competence comes from safety and classifier training data that is overwhelmingly English, so by the same structural argument as 9.3, a GPT-3.5-class trusted monitor that flags suspicious code or content well in English should have higher false-negative rates in isiZulu, Yoruba, or Amharic. No published evaluation yet measures this directly, and the multilingual table we cite in the readings in fact runs the other way: in the Circuit Breakers evaluation (Appendix E, Table 5), undefended 7–8B models show their lowest attack-success rates in Zulu (6.6% for Mistral-7B, 4.6% for Llama-3-8B, against roughly 50% for high-resource languages), and the authors note that the Yong et al. low-resource pattern does not appear in their results. That is the comprehension confound from 9.4 again: a small model that barely understands a request cannot comply with it, and would be little use monitoring it either. So the 10.2 safety numbers are computed on an English testbed, the monitor's competence outside English is unmeasured rather than demonstrated, and the delivered safety of the same protocol for an African-language deployment is unknown, because the evaluations (Session 11) are not multilingual. This is the same fault line as the isiZulu jailbreak (Session 9), now inside the defence itself.

Questions to bring to class

  • Why does concentrating the audit budget on \(T\)'s most-suspicious 2% beat auditing a blind prefix of the same size? What property of \(T\) makes this work even though \(T\) cannot write safe code itself?
  • Defer-to-trusted at the 50% threshold reaches 100% safety but 51% usefulness. When is that trade worth it, and when is trusted editing the better buy?
  • Derive the monitor's collude-or-flag condition \(\mathrm{LR}(s) > r\). What does redaction do to \(\mathrm{LR}(s)\), and what does planting more synthetic backdoors do to \(r\)?
  • Trusted monitoring's safety is computed in English. Sketch how you would measure the safety actually delivered for an isiZulu deployment, and what you would need that does not yet exist.

Readings

Core

• Greenblatt, Shlegeris, Sachan & Roger (2023), "AI Control" (arXiv:2312.06942). §3 for the protocol results above and §3.4.2 for the collusion analysis.

Supplementary

• Zou et al. (2024), "Improving Alignment and Robustness with Circuit Breakers" (arXiv:2406.04313). §3–4 for the representation-level defence; Appendix E, Table 5 for the multilingual results read in the African-lens box.

• Bhatt et al. (2025), "Ctrl-Z: Controlling AI Agents via Resampling" (arXiv:2504.10374). Control protocols on multi-step agentic tasks (BashBench, 257 tasks); resample protocols cut attack success from 58% to 7% at about a 5% usefulness cost.

• Korbak et al. (2025), "How to evaluate control measures for LLM agents?" (arXiv:2504.05259): a proposed trajectory for control evaluations as capabilities grow.
