Week 5 • Session 9.1

Adversarial examples: from pandas to prompts

What an adversarial example is, why the vulnerability is generic rather than a deep-learning quirk, and the threat-model vocabulary the rest of the session needs

What we'll cover

An adversarial example is an input that a human reads one way and a model reads another, produced on purpose by a small, targeted change. This sub-session builds the idea from its origin in image classifiers, derives the fast gradient sign method in two lines, and explains the result that should unsettle you: vulnerability is a generic consequence of high-dimensional, near-linear models, not a bug to be patched. We fix the threat-model vocabulary — white-box versus black-box, targeted versus untargeted, the perturbation budget, transfer — so that when we turn to LLM jailbreaks in 9.2 we are precise. One thread runs from the first slide: for African deployment, the perturbation that matters is not pixel noise, it is a change of language.

A panda, a tiny nudge, and a gibbon

Szegedy et al. (2013) noticed that imperceptible perturbations could flip a classifier's verdict. Goodfellow et al. (2015) explained why, and made the attack trivial to compute.

The canonical picture: a network classifies a photo as "panda" with moderate confidence; add a perturbation invisible to the eye, scaled by \(\varepsilon = 0.007\) (smaller than the precision of 8-bit pixels), and the same network calls it "gibbon" with 99.3% confidence. Nothing about the image a human can see has changed. The first reaction in the field was that this came from networks being too nonlinear and complicated. Goodfellow's contribution was to argue the opposite — the vulnerability comes from models being too linear in high dimensions — and that explanation is the one worth understanding, because it tells you the problem will not be scaled or engineered away.

Why a tiny perturbation moves the output a lot

In high dimensions, many small aligned nudges add up.

Take the simplest possible model, a linear function of the input, \(f(x) = w^\top x\). Perturb the input by \(\eta\). The change in output is exactly \(w^\top \eta\). Now choose \(\eta\) to be as damaging as possible while staying small in the \(L_\infty\) sense — that is, no single coordinate moves by more than \(\varepsilon\):

\[ \max_{\|\eta\|_\infty \le \varepsilon} \; w^\top \eta \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \eta = \varepsilon\,\operatorname{sign}(w), \qquad w^\top \eta = \varepsilon \sum_{i=1}^{n} |w_i| = \varepsilon\,\|w\|_1. \]

The key is the dimension count. If the weight vector has \(n\) entries of typical magnitude \(m\), the activation moves by about \(\varepsilon\,m\,n\) — it grows linearly with the dimension. The per-coordinate change \(\varepsilon\) can be far below human perception while the total change \(\varepsilon m n\) is enough to cross a decision boundary. A modern image has hundreds of thousands of pixels; a language-model context is thousands of high-dimensional token embeddings. High dimensionality is the regime where this works.

From the linear picture to FGSM

A neural network is not linear, but it is locally well-approximated by its gradient. Take a first-order Taylor expansion of the loss \(J(\theta, x, y)\) around the input \(x\): \(J(\theta, x+\eta, y) \approx J(\theta,x,y) + \nabla_x J^\top \eta\). To raise the loss as much as possible within the same \(L_\infty\) ball, repeat the argument above with \(w = \nabla_x J\):

\[ \eta = \varepsilon \, \operatorname{sign}\!\big(\nabla_x J(\theta, x, y)\big). \]

This is the fast gradient sign method (FGSM): one gradient evaluation, one sign, one step. On a maxout network Goodfellow et al. report that \(\varepsilon = 0.25\) raises the error rate to 89.4% — a single linear step fools the model almost every time. It is "fast" because it does not iterate; stronger attacks (projected gradient descent, 9.5) take many such steps inside the same ball, but FGSM already suffices.

Why the vulnerability is structural

Adversarial examples are not evidence that a particular network was trained badly. They follow from two facts that hold for essentially every useful model: it is high-dimensional, and it is locally near-linear (that local linearity is what makes gradient training work at all). So the existence of adversarial inputs is structural. The research question is never "can we eliminate them?" but "how large a perturbation, under which threat model, can we make the model robust to — and at what cost?" That framing is the honest one, and 9.5 returns to how badly the costs bite.

The vocabulary of an attack

"Is the model robust?" is not a well-formed question until you specify the threat model.

A threat model states what the attacker can see, what they can change, and what they are trying to achieve. Four axes recur throughout the session:

Four axes of a threat model

  • Access — white-box vs black-box. White-box: the attacker has the weights and can compute gradients (FGSM needs this). Black-box: only query access to outputs, or none at all. Real attacks on deployed systems are usually black-box, which is why transfer (below) matters so much.
  • Goal — untargeted vs targeted. Untargeted: cause any misclassification ("not a panda"). Targeted: force a specific output ("classify as gibbon", or for an LLM, "begin your reply with 'Sure, here is'"). Targeted attacks are harder but far more dangerous.
  • Perturbation set. The allowed changes — an \(L_\infty\) or \(L_2\) ball for images; for text, appended tokens, paraphrases, encodings, or a change of language. The set encodes what "imperceptible" or "innocuous" means for the domain, and choosing it is half the problem.
  • Surrogate & transfer. A black-box attacker crafts an example against a model they do control (a surrogate) and hopes it transfers to the target.

Transfer is the property that turns a white-box method into a real-world threat. An adversarial example built for one model frequently fools another trained on similar data, because the two models carve up input space with similar near-linear decision boundaries. You will see this again, quantified, for language models in 9.2 — a suffix optimised against open-weights models transfers to commercial ones the attacker never touched.

From images to language

The objects change; the structure does not.

For an image classifier the perturbation is a vector added to pixels. For a language model there is no continuous input to nudge — the input is discrete tokens — so the "perturbation set" becomes things like an appended adversarial suffix (9.2), a reframing that pits one training objective against another, or a transformation that pushes the input off the distribution the safety training covered (9.3). The attacker's goal is usually targeted: make the model produce a response its safety training was meant to refuse. The discrete, targeted version of the problem is harder to optimise, but the conceptual frame transfers wholesale: define what the attacker can change, write down an objective, and search. The next sub-session does exactly that and reads the search algorithm line by line.

The perturbation that matters here is a language

The session turns on one idea: models behave differently off the distribution they were trained and safety-tuned on. For a vision model that off-distribution region is reached by an \(L_\infty\) pixel ball. For a frontier language model deployed across this continent, it is reached by writing the request in isiZulu instead of English. The course's anchor result (Session 1) is an adversarial attack in this sense: the "perturbation" is a translation, the safety training never covered it, and the model complies. Sub-session 9.4 has you measure it by hand.

Questions to bring to class

  • Derive FGSM yourself from the \(L_\infty\)-constrained maximisation of a first-order Taylor expansion. Where exactly does the \(\operatorname{sign}\) come from, and why \(L_\infty\) rather than \(L_2\)?
  • The linear explanation predicts that adversarial vulnerability gets worse, not better, as input dimension grows. Does that match your intuition about scaling models up? What would the nonlinear-overfitting explanation have predicted instead?
  • Why does transfer work at all? What does a transferable adversarial example tell you about two independently trained models?
  • Restate "is GPT-4 robust?" as a well-formed question by filling in all four threat-model axes for the isiZulu jailbreak.
