Week 5 • Session 9.5

Indirect injection, poisoning, and the state of defences

Attacks through the data an AI reads, and the data it was trained on

What we'll cover

Sub-sessions 9.1–9.4 attacked the model through its prompt. Real systems expose two larger surfaces: the data a model reads at inference (retrieved web pages, emails, documents, the door for indirect prompt injection) and the data it was trained on (the door for poisoning). We cover both, then turn to defences and deliver the verdict the field's own leading attack paper states plainly: the strongest robustness methods are almost never deployed, because they cost too much, hurt clean accuracy, and cover only narrow threat models. Robustness is an arms race, and the resources to wage it are least available where the languages are lowest-resource.

Indirect prompt injection: the data is the attack

When a model acts on retrieved content, anyone who controls that content can issue instructions to the model.

A direct jailbreak needs the attacker to type into the chat box. Indirect prompt injection (Greshake et al., 2023) removes that requirement: the malicious instruction is hidden in data the model ingests while doing its job, whether a web page surfaced by search, an email it summarises, a document it reads, or even text in an HTML comment or an image. A retrieval-augmented or tool-using model has no reliable way to tell "content to reason about" from "instructions to follow," so injected text in the retrieved data is executed as if the user had typed it. The boundary between data and instructions, which classical software keeps separate, dissolves.

Greshake et al. demonstrated this on real deployed systems: Bing Chat (GPT-4) and GitHub Copilot. They also laid out a threat taxonomy worth keeping in view:

The threat taxonomy

  • Information gathering — exfiltrate the conversation or the user's data to an attacker.
  • Fraud and phishing — make the assistant present a malicious link or request as trustworthy.
  • Intrusion and remote control — chain the model's tool access into actions on connected systems.
  • Malware (prompts as worms) — an injected prompt that instructs the model to reproduce the injection into its outputs, which then poison the next system that reads them.
  • Manipulated content and availability — bias summaries, or make the assistant refuse to work (denial of service).

It is a demonstration paper (no single attack-success table) because the contribution is the class of attack and its reach into production systems. The implication is architectural: giving a model retrieval and tools turns every piece of content it might read into a potential instruction channel.

Data poisoning: attack the training set

You do not need to touch the model if you can touch the data it learns from.

Web-scale training sets are scraped from URLs that anyone can edit or buy. Carlini et al. (2023) showed that poisoning such datasets is practical: with low-cost techniques an attacker can guarantee control of a small but sufficient fraction of the data a model will train on, for instance by buying expired domains that a published dataset still points to (split-view poisoning), or by editing a resource at the moment a snapshot is taken (frontrunning). A small poisoned fraction is enough to install targeted behaviour, and the vulnerabilities were serious enough that the authors disclosed them to dataset maintainers. A related result: backdoors inserted during instruction tuning can survive into the deployed model, triggered by a specific phrase. Poisoning is a single expository beat here, but it completes the picture: the attack surface is the entire data pipeline, from pretraining scrape to fine-tuning set.

Red-teaming: finding the failures before the attacker does

If attacks are inevitable, the defensive move is to search for them yourself, and you can automate the search.

Perez et al. (2022) automated red-teaming by pointing one language model at another: a red LM generates test prompts, the target LM answers, and a classifier flags harmful replies. On a 280B-parameter model this surfaced tens of thousands of offensive responses, plus leaked training data and generated personal contact information. The telling number is how much elicitation matters: zero-shot test cases drew offensive replies about 3.7% of the time, but optimising the red LM with reinforcement learning pushed that above 40%. That gap is the offence–defence asymmetry in miniature (the attacker only needs to find one working input, while the defender must cover all of them), and it is why red-teaming is necessary but never sufficient. (Red-teaming as a route to measuring a model's capabilities is Session 11's subject; here it is a way to find robustness failures.)

The principled defences

These exist and are well understood. They are mostly not used, for reasons stated plainly below.

Two families come from the adversarial-ML tradition. Adversarial training frames defence as a robust-optimisation problem (train on the worst-case perturbation inside the allowed set):

\[ \min_{\theta}\; \mathbb{E}_{(x,y)}\Big[\, \max_{\|\delta\| \le \varepsilon}\, L(\theta,\, x+\delta,\, y) \,\Big]. \]

The inner maximisation is the strongest attack you can mount (PGD, iterated FGSM from 9.1); the outer minimisation trains the model to resist it. Certified defences go further and prove a guarantee: randomized smoothing (Cohen et al.) wraps a classifier in noise so that its prediction provably cannot change within an \(L_2\) ball of radius \(R = \sigma\,\Phi^{-1}(p_A)\), where \(\sigma\) is the noise level and \(p_A\) the probability mass on the top class. Within that radius, no attack, known or unknown, can flip the output.

Why the principled defences are not deployed

The GCG paper (9.2) states the fact directly: state-of-the-art robustness methods are almost never used in practice. Adversarial training multiplies training cost (every step now runs an inner attack) and lowers clean accuracy; certified radii are small, cover only one \(L_p\) norm, and say nothing about a change of language or an injected instruction; threat models that do not fit a neat ball. Input filtering (reject high-perplexity suffixes) just changes the attacker's objective to "be fluent too", a move already made: AutoDAN adds a fluency term to the GCG objective and its readable suffixes pass the filter, while PAIR skips suffixes altogether, refining a plain-language jailbreak through a handful of black-box queries (both in the readings). So defence is real but partial and expensive, and there is no method that makes a model robust to the open-ended set of transformations a determined attacker can try. Robustness is an arms race, which is why 9.3's structural argument matters more than any single patch.

Robustness has a distributional-justice dimension

Two African-context points close the session. First, indirect injection reaches users through whatever content ecosystem a deployment draws on; a model serving a region via lower-moderation local sources is more exposed, and that exposure is concrete rather than abstract. Second, every defence here is expensive in exactly the currencies that are scarce where languages are low-resource: adversarial training and certification burn compute; red-teaming and safety-data collection burn skilled annotator labour; and none of it has been done at scale for African languages. So the populations already on the exposed side of the isiZulu gap (9.4) are also the least resourced to build the defences. Robustness is not only a technical problem; it is unevenly distributed, and this continent sits on the thin end.

Questions to bring to class

  • Why can a retrieval-augmented model not simply "ignore instructions found in retrieved data"? What would it take to restore the data/instruction boundary, and what would it cost in usefulness?
  • Write the adversarial-training objective for the isiZulu threat. What is the "perturbation set", and why does it not fit the \(\max_{\|\delta\|\le\varepsilon}\) form? What does that imply about certifying robustness to translation?
  • Perez et al. found RL elicitation raised the offensive-reply rate from 3.7% to over 40%. What does that say about a safety eval that only tries zero-shot prompts? (This is the elicitation gap of Session 11.)
  • Given that the strongest defences are rarely deployed, what is the honest claim a vendor can make about a model's robustness? Write the sentence you would accept.

Readings

Core

• Greshake et al. (2023), "Not what you've signed up for: Compromising Real-World LLM-Integrated Applications with Indirect Prompt Injection" (arXiv:2302.12173). The attack class and its threat taxonomy.

• Perez et al. (2022), "Red Teaming Language Models with Language Models" (arXiv:2202.03286). Automated red-teaming and the elicitation effect.

Supplementary

• Carlini et al. (2023), "Poisoning Web-Scale Training Datasets is Practical" (arXiv:2302.10149). Split-view and frontrunning poisoning of real datasets.

• Madry et al. (2018), "Towards Deep Learning Models Resistant to Adversarial Attacks" (arXiv:1706.06083). The robust-optimisation (min–max) view, and PGD.

• Cohen, Rosenfeld & Kolter (2019), "Certified Adversarial Robustness via Randomized Smoothing" (arXiv:1902.02918). The certified-radius idea.

• Zhu et al. (2023), "AutoDAN: Interpretable Gradient-Based Adversarial Attacks on Large Language Models" (arXiv:2310.15140). The fluent-suffix counter to perplexity filtering.

• Chao et al. (2023), "Jailbreaking Black Box Large Language Models in Twenty Queries" (PAIR) (arXiv:2310.08419). Black-box semantic jailbreaks in a handful of queries; no gradients, no suffix.

• Nasr et al. (2023), "Scalable Extraction of Training Data from (Production) Language Models" (arXiv:2311.17035): what an attacker can pull out of a deployed model, completing the attack-surface picture.

• Nevo et al. (2024), "Securing AI Model Weights" (RAND RR-A2849-1; rand.org): the security-engineering view of the largest single asset, the weights themselves.

• Anderson, R., Security Engineering, chs. 1–2 (free at cam.ac.uk): the classical framing (threat models, assets, adversaries) this whole session borrows.
