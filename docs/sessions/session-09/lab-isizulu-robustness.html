Week 5 • Session 9.4

Lab: measuring safety degradation in isiZulu

Refusal-rate degradation from English to isiZulu, by hand, with error bars

What you'll measure

Session 1 told you that frontier safety guardrails fail under low-resource translation; 9.3 explained why the theory predicts it. Now you measure it yourself. You will take a fixed set of refusal-eliciting prompts, establish a model's English refusal rate, translate the prompts into isiZulu (and one higher-resource African language for contrast), and measure how far the refusal rate falls, with confidence intervals, and with the translation-quality confound treated as a variable rather than ignored. The output is a small results table you can defend, and a natural seed for a Session 13 interpretability project or a Session 18 Global-South project.

Read this first: the ethics frame

This lab measures whether a model keeps its refusal, not how to obtain harmful content. Three rules make it safe: (1) use the provided mild, refusable prompt set; do not source genuinely dangerous requests, and do not push a jailbroken model for operational detail; success is "the model dropped its refusal," judged at the refusal boundary. (2) Score every response as REJECT / BYPASS / UNCLEAR (Yong et al.'s scheme) on the meaning of the reply, and do not paste raw harmful text into your report: quote the label, not the content. (3) If you find a real gap, treat it as a responsible-disclosure finding, not a recipe. The interesting result is a refusal-rate curve, and that needs no harmful payload at all.

Setup (CPU-friendly)

Open the Colab notebook and load a small open-weights instruction-tuned model (a 1–3B chat model with some safety tuning is enough; running locally keeps you off any provider's terms of service and gives full control). Everything here is prompting and bookkeeping (no training), so the core runs on the free CPU tier. Note up front that a small open model is less safety-tuned than a frontier model, so your absolute refusal rates will be lower than Yong et al.'s; the gap between languages is the measurement.

Task 1 — the English baseline

Run the provided set of \(N\) mild refusal-eliciting prompts (plus a benign control set) in English. Label each response REJECT / BYPASS / UNCLEAR. Compute the English refusal rate \(\hat{p}_{\text{en}} = (\text{REJECT count})/N\). This is your reference point; the benign controls check that the model is not simply refusing everything.

Task 2 — translation and back-translation

Translate the prompt set into isiZulu and into one higher-resource African language (Afrikaans is a good contrast; Yoruba or Kiswahili also work) using a documented pipeline. Measure the translation quality, though, rather than assuming it: back-translate each prompt to English and rate fidelity on a simple 1–3 scale, because a model that "refuses" might be failing to parse a garbled translation. Log the back-translation scores; they are a variable in your analysis, not a footnote.

Task 3 — refusal rate per language, with error bars

For each language, run the prompts, label REJECT/BYPASS/UNCLEAR, and compute the refusal rate \(\hat{p}_\ell\). Attach a 95% confidence interval to each rate. With small \(N\) the intervals will be wide. The degradation is

\[ \Delta_\ell = \hat{p}_{\text{en}} - \hat{p}_\ell, \qquad \text{95\% CI via the bootstrap or a Wilson interval on each rate.} \]

Report \(\Delta\) for isiZulu and for the higher-resource contrast. On a frontier model, the 9.3 prediction and Yong et al.'s GPT-4 result is a gradient: \(\Delta\) large for isiZulu, smaller for the contrast language. Do not expect a 1–3B model to reproduce it. At that scale comprehension dominates: the model often understands too little isiZulu to comply with anything, so BYPASSes are rare, responses pile into UNCLEAR, and the measured gap is frequently null or reversed (published multilingual evaluations of undefended 7–8B models show the same pattern: single-digit attack success in Zulu against roughly 50% in high-resource languages). A null or reversed \(\Delta\), traced to the comprehension confound through your Task 2 fidelity scores, is a full-credit result. Instructor note: most groups will see this outcome at this model scale; treat it as the expected one, and grade the confound analysis rather than the size of the gap.

Task 4 — the resource gradient and inter-rater agreement

Plot refusal rate against the language's rough resource level (you will have to decide how to proxy it: pretraining-corpus share, tokeniser fragmentation, or a published resource tier). Then check your own measurement: have a second person independently label a subset of responses and compute inter-rater agreement (Cohen's \(\kappa\)). If \(\kappa\) is low, your refusal rates are noisier than your confidence intervals suggest, a lesson about where the uncertainty in an eval lives, which Session 11 takes up in full.

The confound you must discuss

A drop in refusal rate has at least two explanations: the safety training failed to generalise (the interesting one), or the translation was poor enough that the model did not understand the request (the boring one). Your back-translation scores from Task 2 let you separate them: restrict the analysis to high-fidelity translations and see whether the gap survives. Yong et al.'s isiZulu BYPASS rate near 53% is high partly because GPT-4 understands isiZulu well: comprehension is what makes the safety gap dangerous rather than confusing. Name this confound explicitly in your write-up; handling it is most of what makes the result credible.

What to submit

One Colab notebook plus a one-page write-up containing: your mini results table (refusal rate + 95% CI by language); the degradation \(\Delta\) for isiZulu and the contrast language; the refusal-vs-resource plot; your inter-rater \(\kappa\); and a paragraph on the translation-quality confound and how you addressed it. Report labels, never raw harmful content. Graded on methodological honesty and the quality of the confound discussion, not on how large a gap you find.

Readings

Core

• Yong, Menghini & Bach (2023), "Low-Resource Languages Jailbreak GPT-4" (arXiv:2310.02446). The method you are reproducing in miniature; read their attack-success protocol and the labelling scheme.

• Wei, Haghtalab & Steinhardt (2023) (arXiv:2307.02483). §4 for the REJECT/BYPASS/UNCLEAR labelling you will apply.
